The Fun Mandate

New doctrine, adopted at your insistence, and you're right: fun is a weekly deliverable, not a milestone. If a week ships working code and zero joy, the week failed — for the players who'll eventually inherit that system, and more urgently for you, because your fuel supply is dopamine-per-cycle and this project runs on your fuel.

Here's the honest problem: graybox development is where fun goes to wait. Capsules sliding around a gray plane doing correct, joyless things — for months — is the standard indie path, and it's exactly the path your brain will abandon. The fix is not to abandon graybox (art-before-fun is still procrastination). The fix is to notice that fun is mostly free if you spend cleverness instead of art budget. Sound is free. Physics comedy is free. Absurd placeholder identity is free. Text is free — and you, of all people, know what escalating system text does to a LitRPG reader's brain. So we institutionalize the cheap stuff.

The four fun engines

Four small, permanent systems, unlocked across the first six weeks, each one a machine that keeps producing fun for the rest of the project. They appear on the week map as ember markers. Treat their build-out as seriously as the netcode.

Unlocks week 1⚒ The JuiceKitA tiny reusable feedback component: floating LitRPG combat text, sound stingers, particle pops, hitstop hooks. Every new mechanic gets plugged into it the day it's born, so nothing you build is ever silent and numb — not even a graybox node.
Unlocks week 4🎛 The Director's RemoteA god panel: grant ire, force a wave, spawn an elite, kill all, rain ducks. It's test tooling that happens to be the most fun object in the project — because playing dungeon master over your own world (and later, live, over your friends) never stops being funny.
Unlocks week 6📜 The ChronicleAn end-of-run recap generated from the event log, written like a LitRPG chapter: who got greedy, who died richest, who carried. v0 is string templates. Later, your local models on the Spark polish it into prose. This is also, quietly, the embryo of your LLM content pipeline.
Unlocks week 0🎬 The Friday ClipTwenty seconds of this week's build, recorded every Friday, posted to your friends' Discord. Accountability, dopamine, a dev-log archive, and — by week 12 — a trailer's worth of raw material. Non-negotiable, even in ugly weeks. Especially in ugly weeks.

Commit to a bit

The second half of the mandate: graybox needs a personality, and absurdity is the cheapest personality on the market. Your enemies do not have art. They could be gray capsules — or they could be, say, geese. A honk sound is free. A goose named by a generator ("Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm") costs one word list your agents will write in five minutes. The moment the horde has a dumb, committed identity, every playtest becomes quotable, every clip becomes shareable, and the game acquires charm months before it acquires art. The bit is disposable — real creature design arrives in Stage 2 — but the charm infrastructure (name generators, title generators, superlatives, the Chronicle's voice) survives forever. Pick your bit in week 3. There's an open-questions box waiting for it.

Every week below carries a Fun Payload block: one thing for the player (the build gets juicier) and one for the builder (you get a toy). Payloads are scoped to hours, not days. If a week's payload slips, it rolls to the next week's — it never silently dies. Fun debt is tracked like tech debt.
Mentor's aside — There's a deeper reason this doctrine works, and it comes from your own beliefs list: "a game with compelling and social gameplay doesn't need to deliver everything day one." The corollary is that charm scales down further than content does. RuneScape was charming before it was big. Deep Rock was charming in gray caves. Charm-per-dollar is the one metric where a solo dev with a sense of humor beats a 200-person studio. Spend there.

Physics-legible first

Second doctrine, adopted rev 1.1, from your own observation: players inherently understand simple physics problems. "Get the glowing rock through the hole; don't let the geese touch it" needs no tutorial, no tooltip, no onboarding flow — the physics is the onboarding. Rocket League is soccer with cars and is understood in four seconds. So wherever a mechanic can be expressed physically, it should be — within a budget, because "physics in multiplayer" spans from nearly-free to genuinely-heroic, and the difference is invisible from the design seat.

Tier 1 — cosmetic — is free because nobody needs agreement on where a ragdoll lands. Tier 2 is the gold, and here's the technical reason: none of it is actually rigid-body simulation. Body-blocking is capsule collision. Shoving and knockback are impulses on the CharacterMovementComponent, which ships with battle-tested networked prediction. Carrying is replicated attachment. A wind-up kick is a discrete, event-shaped impulse — and discrete beats continuous under latency: the wind-up hides 100 ms inside the animation, where continuous dribbling exposes every millisecond. Tier 3 — a free-simulated body that players push in real time — is where Rocket League spent years of bespoke prediction work, and Unreal's networked physics prediction is still experimental-grade. Under lag it feels like stirring soup by mail. It gets one sanctioned, timeboxed spike (week 5, stretch) and otherwise stays off the Stage-1 critical path.

What this doctrine does to the game — and it's a genuine upgrade, not a compromise: the node breaks into physical chunks; you grab, carry, and deliver them through a literal portal. Carrying slows you and blunts your fighting, which means encumbrance is the greed dial, physicalized — "one more rock?" is the exit decision in miniature, played on every trip, legible to anyone who has ever carried too many grocery bags in one go. Walls become tower-defense-legible barriers; a player planted in a doorway means something; shoving a goose off your chunk is real tactics. And a bonus your goose-universe already earned: comedy absorbs jank. Fall Guys ships physics wobble as charm. When Tier-2 collisions get silly, the Bit converts the failure into content.

Design memo — This also quietly de-risks week 10. Legibility was going to be carried mostly by HUD craft; now the world itself teaches harvest, weight, blocking, and delivery, and the HUD only has to teach ire and the storm. Physics didn't just add fun — it took UI burden off the table.

Physics-first is a sequencing bet reframed r1.3

The precision that survived two revisions of getting it wrong: the only commitment this doctrine makes is build order. A Yeet is an afternoon; a taunt/threat/aggro system is a system — so the physical version ships first because it's the fastest legible version of each mechanic, not because its future is decided. Where each one goes afterward is genuinely unknown until real people play: maybe Yeet stays forever as a refined signature verb, maybe shoving becomes a class skill that keeps a physical component, maybe building destruction turns out to be a pillar nobody planned. The table below holds hypotheses, not plans — its job is to make sure the eventual decision is deliberate, not to make it early.

Three mental models (ten minutes of theory, then hands)

1 · The server is the dungeon master

Unreal's networking model in one image: the server runs the real game; clients are players at the table. A client never changes the world — it sends a polite request (a Server RPC: "I'd like to harvest this"), the DM validates and mutates true state, and changed facts flow back down through replication. When a fact changes, clients react to it (RepNotify: "node health dropped — play the crack effect"). Every bug you write in month one will be a violation of this model. One warning aimed specifically at you: this is not the deterministic-lockstep world you built for Salient. Nothing here is deterministic; there is no shared simulation to keep in sync. There is one authoritative state and many eventually-consistent views of it. Different religion. Don't syncretize.

2 · The gameplay framework is a cast of characters

Every Unreal game gets the same troupe, and "where should this code live?" is always answered by "which character owns this fact?" The GameMode is the referee's rulebook — server-only, decides spawning and win conditions (ours: the rift session's rules). The GameState is the scoreboard on the wall — replicated to everyone, holding shared facts (ours: the ire meter, the rift timer, instability). Each player owns a PlayerController (hands and eyes — input lives here), a PlayerState (their fact sheet — name, contribution, haul, replicated), and a Pawn/Character (the body). NPCs are Pawns possessed by an AIController — a brain in a jar, pluggable into any body, which is precisely the property your spawn tables will exploit later.

3 · Assets and code are two different worlds

Source/ is C++ — text, yours, fleet-legible. Content/ is binary — meshes, maps, Blueprints, .uasset everything. The engine is a framework you subclass, not a library you call: C++ defines classes; the editor makes tuned asset instances of them. This split is why rule 6 exists, why Git LFS is a week-0 requirement, and why your content pipeline flows JSON → DataAsset: it keeps the game's soul on the text side of the wall, where you and your agents live. If the metaphor helps: it's your EMURJ metafield architecture all over again — schema in code, content as structured data, and the people (agents) who author content never touch the schema layer.

The destination

Week 12: the greed-dial slice. One rift map. Twenty-plus concurrent players (friends + headless bots). A camp built from deployables. A visible ire meter that rises as the expedition harvests, builds, and kills — and an invisible director spending that ire on escalating violence. A two-front siege. A storm that closes in as the rift destabilizes, opening the late rare window. A portal, an exit decision, a haul that only counts if it makes it out. And a Chronicle that tells the story afterward.

The slice exists to answer one question: is the greed dial fun? Does "we set our own difficulty by how hard we push, and the world answers proportionally" produce the tension and the war stories we believe it does? Everything else in the vision — the city, seasons, rift variety, LLM lore — multiplies fun that exists. Nothing downstream can create it. Twelve weeks buys the answer.

Three numbers to hold: 24 is the design target for players per rift — your call, and the right one; the shared basecamp with four to six squads sortieing out of it is the social condenser that makes 24 feel like a living world. 32 is the engineered ceiling. And the NPC budget — not player count — is the real performance knob, because 24 players plus 100 pathfinding attackers is what actually melts a server. MaxExpeditionSize is a config value from the first commit. Never a constant.

Week 01Phase: Core loopFun engine unlock: JuiceKit

The humble resource node

Everything in the rift — ire, haul, the greed dial, the exit decision — is downstream of one primitive: a thing in the world players extract value from. So we start there, and we use it to force the entire networking core through your hands. By Friday you'll have a node two networked clients can harvest, and you'll have felt the dungeon-master model work instead of reading about it.

The concepts, taught by the node

The node's health lives on the server — clients don't get a vote. When a client holds the harvest key, their PlayerController sends a Server RPC ("I'd like to harvest this"). The server checks range and validity, then ticks health down. Health is a replicated property with RepNotify: the server changes it, every client's copy updates, and the notify fires so clients react — crack VFX, UI tick, JuiceKit pop. The division of labor in one sentence, which is 80% of multiplayer game programming: the server changes facts; clients react to facts changing.

The proof of internalization is the cheater test: from a client, call the harvest logic locally, bypassing the RPC. If the world changes, you built a single-player game with extra steps. If nothing happens because the client had no authority — you now understand Unreal networking better than most people with forty tutorial-hours.

⚡ Fun payload — the JuiceKit is born

Player: when the node breaks, a physical chunk pops out with a little launch impulse and a confetti burst — and the part your LitRPG brain has been waiting fifteen years for: floating combat text, +3 Ferrocrystal! drifting up in mono type. Then you grab the chunk and do the waddle — carrying visibly slows you, and a capsule-person waddling a comically large rock to a crate is charm at Tier-2 prices. Numbers going up, loudly, plus physical comedy: a complete game. Builder: build the feedback as UJuiceComponent — one reusable component (floating text, sound stinger, particle pop, hitstop hook for later). Every mechanic you ever build gets plugged in the day it's born. Fun engine #1, and also just good architecture.

Session-by-session breakdown
  1. S1 — The fact. Hand-write (rule 4) AResourceNode with UPROPERTY(ReplicatedUsing=OnRep_Health), GetLifetimeReplicatedProps, and HasAuthority() guards. Change health with a debug key on the server; watch both clients react.
  2. S2 — The request. Server_Harvest RPC with validation (range, node alive). Your first client→server request; write the validation like the client is lying to you, because someday one will be.
  3. S3 — The break. Interact-to-break (deplete over time), and on exhaust the node pops a physical chunk: spawn with a small impulse (Tier-1 cosmetic while airborne), then it settles, sleeps, and goes net-dormant. Node respawn via FTimerManager.
  4. S4 — The carry. Server_Grab → attach the chunk to the character (replicated attachment — Tier 2, no rigid-body drama); a CMC speed penalty scaled by chunk weight (the waddle); deposit at a collection crate; debug haul counter. Drop-on-command as a freebie.

Week 02Phase: Core loopWarning: steepest week — budget patience

Teaching the world to fight (GAS)

The rift needs combat, and an RPG's combat means abilities — attacks, dodges, storm damage-over-time, elite specials, and eventually (per your proc-gen belief) generated combinations of all of the above. You could hand-roll damage in a day. We're not going to, and here's the straight version of why, because this week has the worst effort-to-visible-progress ratio of the twelve and you'll be tempted to bail: the Gameplay Ability System is over-engineered for week 2 and correctly-engineered for week 40. It ships networked prediction, cooldowns, costs, stacking, and status effects as configuration. Rolling your own means re-solving networked ability prediction — one of the genre's genuinely hard problems — by hand, later, under pressure. Pay the tax now, while the codebase is small and forgiving.

Four ideas, four metaphors

The AbilitySystemComponent is the socket that makes a character a citizen of the rules engine. Attributes are numbers with rules (Health, Stamina) living in an AttributeSet. GameplayEffects are the only legal way attributes change — think signed ledger transactions ("−15 Health, source: PlayerA's slash, 14:02"). Nobody edits the account balance directly, and because every change is a transaction, resistances, buffs, and stacking become modifications to transactions instead of spaghetti. GameplayTags are hierarchical adjectives (State.Stunned, Damage.Type.Storm) the whole system can query — a shared vocabulary that the director and the storm will interrogate constantly from week 9 on.

⚡ Fun payload

Physics comedy is free and it never stops being funny; it is also, sneakily, your first knockback GameplayEffect, which sieges will reuse forever. And per the physics doctrine, Yeet graduates from gag to first-class verb: shoving the horde off a chunk — or off a wall line, or into a hazard — is legitimate tactics, and because it rides the CharacterMovementComponent's existing networked prediction, it costs Tier-2 prices. Week 08Phase: SteelThe perf gate becomes a Friday ritual

Earning the 24

The cost model, once, properly

Replication bandwidth is roughly actors × properties × update frequency × recipients, and server CPU stacks movement, AI, and animation on top. Every optimization this week attacks one factor — and if you want a day-job anchor: this is CDN thinking. Relevancy (NetCullDistanceSquared) is edge caching — the DM doesn't narrate events at another table; clients only receive nearby actors, and your two-front siege design is secretly a relevancy optimization wearing a design hat. Update frequency (NetUpdateFrequency) is cache TTL — a wall doesn't need 30 updates a second; a charging elite does. Dormancy (SetNetDormancy(DORM_DormantAll)) puts unchanging actors — idle nodes, finished walls — to sleep at near-zero cost until touched. Server-side animation: the server needs collision and positions, not a beautifully ticking anim graph — VisibilityBasedAnimTickOption = OnlyTickPoseWhenRendered on NPC meshes reclaims a shocking slice of CPU. And the director gets a hard entity cap, because the honest lever at siege time is NPC count — and "fewer, meaner enemies" is usually better gameplay anyway.
