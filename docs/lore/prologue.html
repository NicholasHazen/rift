One More Rock

Every crew has this story off somebody. This is the one they have it off.

The door was eating a week's wages every quarter hour, and Marn kept the count.

She kept all the counts. That was what her articles said: Marnie Ossel, counter, one share and a quarter. Every chunk that had come out of the ground on this charter had crossed her scale, had its grade guessed and its weight chalked on the tally board that now leaned against the doorframe with its numbers all but settled. It had been a good run. Careful, quiet, and good. Two dozen under articles had come through the door at dawn, and the board said every one of them was going home worth more than the year they'd had before.

Around her the camp was coming down the way a camp should: extractors unbolted and slung, wall panels dropped flat and stacked, four squads moving the haul to the threshold in relays while the fifth stood a line nobody had needed all afternoon. The door stood in the middle of it and drank. It was a Charter engine the size of a hay wagon, brass where it faced the weather and black iron where it faced the hole it held open, and the hole was the only honest thing in the rift: a seam of topside daylight, wrong-colored against the storm, with mud on the far side of it that belonged to a world that would still exist tomorrow. Wick fed it from the fuel crate like a man stoking a stove in a house he didn't own — a brick of refined glim every quarter hour, ember-colored, each one stamped with the Charter's mark and worth more than the man feeding it.

The storm had been closer every time she looked. That was the thing about a rift at the end of its day that no amount of telling prepared you for: the weather didn't come toward you, the world went toward it. The horizon had been a range of gray hills at dawn. By noon it was a wall of moving slate. Now it was near enough that she could see the lightning inside it going the wrong way, upward, like something swimming.

"Board's closed," she told the relay hands, and chalked the last line, and that should have been the run.

Then Haldane came back from the north seam.

He came through the camp at a walk. Twenty years under articles taught a chief that much — nothing spends faster than a crew that sees its chief run. But his right arm hung stiff at his side, cradling something against his ribs the way you'd carry a lantern in wind, and the squads stopped their relays to watch him pass, so it spent anyway.

He put it on her scale without a word.

It was the size of a fist and the color of the storm. Not gray — the color the storm actually was when you stopped calling it gray: slate shot through with that wrong-way lightning, a blue like deep ice over something burning. Common glim burned ember; you could warm your hands at a raw seam of it. This sat on the scale and made the air around it cold, and it was heavier than it had any right to be — she felt it through the scale's arm, a weight like a grudge. She had seen the grade twice in her life, both times behind Charter glass with a clerk watching her hands.

"Widowglim," she said, because somebody had to.

"Closing-grade luminous," Haldane said, which was the same word with the Charter's gloves on. "The north seam bloomed, Marn. The whole face of it, not an hour ago. It's coming up out of the rock like frost coming up a window." He was not an excitable man; she had watched him tally a cave-in in the same voice he used for weather. His voice now was very level, and his eyes were not. "Weigh it."

She weighed it. She did the sum twice because the first time she did not believe it, and then she chalked it on the closed board, one line under all the others, and the line was worth more than the board.

Wick left the door to its stoking long enough to look. He was the doorwright; the fuel math lived in his head the way the tallies lived in hers, and she watched him run it, lips moving, eyes going from the chunk to the crate to the storm.

"Say it out loud," Haldane said.

"Crate's at nine bricks. Door drinks one a quarter hour, and it drinks faster the nearer the storm leans — the rift bears down on the hole when it's dying, that's just what a rift does. Call it two hours of open door if the leaning stays polite, and it won't. And when the crate's dry, chief, that hole closes like an eye, whether we're through it or not."

"The seam's forty minutes out. Loaded, an hour back."

"Then you're asking the storm's permission, not mine."

The rift answers exactly as loudly as it's asked. Every miner had that proverb off their mother or their first chief; Marn had it off both. All day they had asked quiet — careful harvest on the marked veins, no second extractor where one would serve — and the rift had answered quiet: small waves at the walls, geese (no crew will call the rift's collectors anything grander) put down at a rate a resting squad could nap through. But the ledger was still open. That was the other half of the proverb, the half people left off because it didn't comfort: the rift kept its own count, and it never closed its board, and everything went in it. Every chunk cut. Every wall standing its minutes. Every goose put down at the line was an entry in the rift's ledger same as it was a line in hers — you could not fight the rift's answer without asking it a louder question. She had been a counter eleven years and she believed in very little, but she believed in that, because she had watched two ledgers all her working life and only one of them ever missed an entry, and it was never the rift's.

"We're made," she said. "Board's closed. Every share on this charter goes home fat. That's the whole job, Haldane. That's the job, done."

"One squad. One trip. No second cuts — we take what's standing proud of the face and we walk away from the rest. Marn—" and here his level voice did the thing she would hear for the rest of her life; it went gentle. "That seam pays off every article on this crew. Not the run — the articles. Sef's whole debt clears. I finish the boat. Whatever it is you're going home to be, you go home and be it and never walk through a door again. One more hour of work, and nobody on these articles ever needs a rift again."

That was the pull of it, and she wanted it set down somewhere that it was not stupidity, because everything he said was true. The board was closed and the sums were right there and the bloom was real. The trap in a closing rift is not that it lies to you. It is that at the very end, it finally starts telling the truth.

They went out seven: Haldane; Marn; Sef and Brann for haulers; three of Ostergard's squad for the guns. The rest struck what remained of camp and moved the haul to the threshold, and Wick stood at his crate and did not wish them luck, which from Wick was a speech.

The north seam was everything he'd said. The face had split in the bloom the way ice splits, and the widowglim stood proud of it in fists and knuckles, storm-colored, lit from inside, cold. Nobody spoke while they worked. There is a kind of wealth that shuts a crew up, and it is not the comfortable kind.

She kept the count while they cut, because she was the counter, and so she can tell you exactly what the rift charged. Four minutes for the first wave to find them — small, and Ostergard's three put it down almost politely, every shot its own entry in the ledger. Nine minutes for the second, and it was not small. By the third she had stopped counting waves and started counting what was left of the light.

"Load," Haldane said. "Load and walk."

You cannot swing anything worth swinging with forty pounds of the world strapped across your back. The rift knows it. Marn had come to believe the rift counted on it — that this was the oldest trick in its book, older than doors: letting a crew convert itself, one strap and one buckle at a time, from seven fighters into seven targets that clinked. They walked. The waves came at the walk's pace, out of the storm side first and then, the way it always goes when the ledger runs hot, out of both.

Sef went down at the creekbed, half a mile out from camp. She did not see what did it. In the closing light you never see what does it — only shapes where the storm-light fails, and after, the geese-noise moving off satisfied. He was a big man who had carried more than any of them, and everything he had carried lay where he dropped it, storm-colored in the creek mud, still lit from inside. His kit they took; that was articles — a man's gear goes home to his people. His load stayed. Six could not carry seven shares, and there was no way to say so out loud that wasn't arithmetic, so nobody said it, and the arithmetic lay there glowing in the mud behind them.

The camp, when they reached it, was gone — struck, crossed, done, the last relays through. Only the door still stood, and Wick at his crate, and the crate was not at nine bricks. The storm had stopped being a wall and become a ceiling. The horizon was a thing she could have hit with a slung stone. The door's seam of daylight had gone narrow, an eye squinted against wind, and it flinched at every brick Wick fed it.

"Through!" Wick was screaming before they were in earshot, had maybe been screaming a while. "Everything through, it's leaning, it's leaning—"

They put the widowglim through the eye the old way, sling by sling, thrown — freight crosses plain, it's the living that need the door's whole attention. Brann crossed. Ostergard's three crossed. Marn stood at the threshold with the last sling in her fists and topside mud six inches past her boots, and looked back for her chief, and her chief was not behind her.

He was ten yards out, in the flinching light, turned the wrong way. Turned toward the creekbed.

"It's half a mile," she said, or screamed; she has never been sure which. "It's just sitting there—"

"One more rock," Haldane said. Level. Gentle, even. The way a man says one more hand, one more glass, one more year. Twenty years under articles, and no fool lasts ten — so it was never foolishness she watched go jogging back into that light with an empty sling swinging. She has had years since to find the word for what it was. She has stopped looking. The rift found it first, and the rift does not lend out its ledger.

Wick held the door to the last brick. He held it past the last brick — held it on whatever a doorwright holds a door with when the fuel is gone and the world is leaning on the hole, screaming at her the whole time to cross, cross, cross — and she stood in the eye with the mud of two worlds on her boots until the eye began to close regardless of anything anyone had paid it. Then Wick's fist was in her collar, and topside took them both, and behind them the seam of daylight shut the way a tally line ends: cleanly, and forever, and exactly on its sum.

Nothing has ever come back out of a closed rift. Ask the Charter what lies behind the last brick and you get a clerk's shrug and the standing rate for widows. Ask a crew and you get the proverb — the whole proverb this time, both halves. The rift answers exactly as loudly as it's asked. And it always, always balances its book.

The assay house took the haul at dawn, and the Chronicle was read at noon, the way it is always read: crew and kin on benches, the clerk winding the machine, the machine the size of a hatbox saying the run back in its dry little voice. The Chronicle does not grieve and is not permitted to lie; that is why the shares are paid on its say and nobody else's. It named every cut and every kill and every wall that stood its minutes. It gave Sef Andriessen his deeds, his debt-clearance, and the line every crew waits on: taken by the rift, held in the count. And at the end — because it hears everything said within its keeping, and pays out the last words of the dead as faithfully as their wages — it gave them Haldane.

One more rock, it said, in the voice that is nobody's voice. Then the sum of a man, which was long, and generous, and ten yards short of the door.

His share came to Marn to carry; that is a counter's last duty under articles. The Charter had cut and finished the bloom's first stone by then, and his portion of it sat in her palm like a piece of the storm with the swimming light still inside: closing-grade luminous, said the assay slip, but nobody under articles has ever called it that. She walked it up the hill to a house with a half-planked boat in the yard, and put it on the table, in the quiet, in front of the woman the grade is named for.

It is the heaviest stone the rift makes. Ilsa Haldane lifted it like it weighed nothing at all.
