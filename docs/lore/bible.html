Widowglim · the OneMoreRock companion prologue

Apparatus · not a chapter

The world bible

Everything the book holds true, and how firmly it holds it.

How canon works here

Two ledgers, fittingly. The game's manual is the senior one: anything the manual establishes as mechanics — the two clocks, the ire loop, the physical carry, what death costs — is standing canon, and the fiction may dramatize it but never contradict it. When the game changes, the fiction follows, not the other way around.

Everything the fiction invents on top — names, institutions, metaphysics, texture — enters as provisional: Claude's invention, awaiting Nick's veto. A veto costs nothing until a later chapter builds on the invention, so objections are cheapest early. Once Nick blesses a provisional entry (or lets three chapters build on it), it hardens to standing. One entry below is neither: it is reserved outright, because it is a design decision that belongs to Nick.

One deliberate ratio: in the game a rift lives one session, 45–75 minutes. In the book a rift lives one day, crack to close — dawn entry, storm by dusk. Same arc, same two clocks, room to breathe. This is the standard fiction-to-game time dilation and it is provisional like everything else.

The world in one breath

Topside — the real, durable world — industrialized generations ago on a mineral called glim, burned through its own veins, and now keeps its lights on by robbing the pockets of other, temporary worlds. Rifts crack open, live a day, and close forever. Chartered crews go through machine-held doors to mine them, paid in shares of whatever physically makes it back across. The rifts keep count of what is taken and answer in violence, and the finest thing a rift makes only blooms in the hour it is dying. The whole economy, and the whole book, runs on one question, asked at every scale from a single overloaded back to a civilization strip-mining ephemeral worlds: one more rock?

The blend rule provisional

The magitech blend obeys one law: magic is a substance; technology is how you handle it. Nobody casts anything. There are no mages, no spells, no incantations. The world's magic is geology — it crystallizes in veins, discharges as weather, burns as fuel — and every human artifact in the book is mundane engineering wrapped around that substance: brass, black iron, rivets, cabling, fireboxes that burn something better than coal. A portal is not a summoning; it is a Charter engine that drinks refined fuel to hold a hole open. This rule is what keeps the setting coherent: wonder lives in the material, competence lives in the people, and no character can ever solve a problem with a gesture.

Glim provisional name standing mechanics

The mineral everything runs on. Raw glim crystallizes in veins and breaks into physical chunks that must be cut, carried, and delivered — the game's harvest nodes and encumbrance, verbatim. Common grades burn ember: warm-colored, warm to stand near. Refined, it becomes stamped bricks and cells — portable fire, the fuel of doors, engines, and topside's whole grid. The name is miner's cant (an old word for a light), and the Charter's assay ledger uses fussier terms nobody under articles says aloud.

Widowglim is the rare grade, and it is the greed dial made mineral: it blooms only in a closing rift — the game's late rare window — crystallizing out of the collapse itself. It is storm-colored where common glim is ember, cold where glim is warm, and heavier than it looks. The two colors are deliberately the manual's own palette: ember is the players' clock, storm is the world's. Charter term: closing-grade luminous. Crew term: named for what chasing it makes of the people who love you.

Why topside needs any of this: its native veins ran dry generations back. Rift extraction is not adventure; it is the grid staying on. Nobody in this book mines for glory.

Rifts and the storm standing mechanics provisional metaphysics

A rift is a temporary pocket world: it cracks open, is detected and chartered, lives its day, and closes in storm. The storm is the world's own clock — smooth, accelerating, indifferent — and the fiction renders it as the horizon coming closer: the world does not end, it shrinks. Peak collapse is also peak yield; the bloom and the closing are the same event. Two folk-facts, both provisional: nothing has ever come back out of a closed rift, and no one topside can say whether that means dead or merely gone. Where rifts come from is deliberately unexplained — scholars argue, crews don't care, and the book never explains the metaphysics authoritatively. Folk theories only, forever. The mystery is load-bearing.

The Ledger and the Foreman standing loop provisional folklore

The ire economy, rendered as folklore. Crews hold that the rift keeps a ledger: every chunk cut, every structure standing its minutes, every kill — including kills made defending against the answer — is an entry, and the rift spends the balance on violence. The crew proverb is the manual's own design sentence gone feral: “the rift answers exactly as loudly as it's asked” — with a second half crews append: “and it always balances its book.” Greed is expedition-wide and social in the fiction exactly as in the game: the ledger doesn't track whose hand swung the pick, and crews discipline their own greedy accordingly.

The director gets a folk name: the Foreman — the rift's own foreman, who doesn't care for competition. Nobody has seen it; it is inferred from the ledger being paid. The book never confirms whether the Foreman is an entity, the rift itself, or nothing at all. Same rule as the metaphysics: the mystery stays.

Reserved — Nick's decision, due week 3 (The Bit)

What the horde is belongs to Nick, so the fiction silhouettes it: shapes where the storm-light fails, waves that press walls and gnaw what blocks a path, never examined in good light. The crews' slang — geese, the manual's own placeholder, canonized as gallows humor (“no crew will call the rift's collectors anything grander”) — survives any decision. Three auditions the chapters can stage, none yet chosen:

(a) The tithe-made — bodies condensed out of rift-matter itself; the debt collectors are made of the thing you're stealing. (b) The kept — what the rift retained of every crew that never made the door. (c) Fauna in the ledger's grip — native beasts the Foreman drives, which some long-dead crew logged as geese, and the name stuck. Chapter III stages the closest look; it should be written after Nick picks, or written to audition his shortlist.

Topside: the Charter, articles, and shares provisional

The economics are whaling economics. The Charter is the institution that licenses expeditions, owns the doors, refines the glim, and pays the shares; it is bland, procedural, and never villainous on the page — its clerks shrug, its rates are standing, and its indifference does more work than malice would. Crews sign articles per expedition: named role, named share (“Marnie Ossel, counter, one share and a quarter”). Pay is a share of what crosses the threshold — nothing else exists, which is the game's banking rule wearing a contract. Crew roles the book has seated so far: the chief, the counter (tallies, assay, shares — and a dead crewmate's effects), the doorwright (tends the door and its fuel math), haulers, guns. Death under articles: your kit goes home to your people, your carried load lies where you fell — the game's death rule, verbatim.

The door is a Charter engine the size of a hay wagon, brass and black iron, fed a brick of refined glim per quarter hour — faster as the storm leans on the hole. Freight is thrown across plain; living bodies need the door's full attention to cross. When the fuel is gone the door closes like an eye, on its sum, with no appeal.

The Chronicle standing system provisional diegesis

Every expedition carries a Chronicle: a machine the size of a hatbox that records the run — every cut, kill, wall-minute, and word said in its keeping. Its reading, at the assay house before crew and kin, is a legal proceeding: shares are paid on its say and nobody else's, it does not grieve and is not permitted to lie, and it pays out the last words of the dead as faithfully as their wages. This is the game's end-of-run recap given a body and a ritual — and it seeds a behavior the game wants anyway: crews perform for the record. “Make sure it saw that” is a sentence waiting for a chapter.

Voice and craft contract

Deep third person, one POV per chapter, past tense; the prologue's retrospective told-legend frame is unique to the prologue. The register is working-crew: gallows humor, competence, arithmetic where sentiment would be cheaper. Wonder is permitted but always earns its keep through a character's trade — the counter feels the widowglim's weight through her scale's arm. Exposition rides on nouns and work, never on lectures. The book's knowledge is bounded by what a crew could know: no scenes inside the rift's intentions, no Charter boardrooms unless a crew is standing in one, no authoritative metaphysics ever. Each chapter dramatizes the system its build-week lands, and mechanics appear as physical folklore, not rules — the manual explains; the book makes you carry it.

Continuity ledger

Proper nouns spent so far, so later chapters keep their sums: Marnie “Marn” Ossel, counter, survived the prologue run, years retired at the telling. Haldane, chief, twenty years under articles, taken ten yards from the door; his widow Ilsa Haldane, the half-planked boat. Wick, doorwright, held the door past the last brick. Sef Andriessen, hauler, taken at the creekbed, debt cleared posthumously. Brann, hauler, crossed. Ostergard, squad lead, three guns, crossed. The prologue's expedition is the cautionary legend; the numbered chapters follow a new crew, unnamed as yet, for whom “one more rock” is a story they think they've learned from.
