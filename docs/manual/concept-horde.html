Brains on the carpet

When to (re)read this: before week 3's first session — before the first goose gets a brain — then again when week 5's walls start editing the carpet, and again before week 8 sends eighty geese at one wall line.

An NPC in this game is three cheap parts: a rented brain, a disposable body, and a pre-computed answer to the question "where can feet go?" This page assembles one goose from those parts — possession, the carpet, the flowchart — then scales it up: the tap that pours bodies, the doorway where a player becomes terrain, and the one component swap that keeps eighty geese from becoming a mosh pit. Everything here comes with its price tag attached, because the horde is the server's biggest bill.

The brain in the jar

Start with a sentence you already know from the player's side: a PlayerController possesses a Character — hands and eyes given a body. An NPC is the same sentence with the subject swapped: an AIController possesses a Pawn. The controller is a brain in a jar, pluggable into any body. It is not the goose; it is the thing that decides for the goose. The body carries the mesh, the capsule, the attributes. The brain carries the decisions. Where the player half of this troupe lives, and which character owns which fact → concept-anatomy.html#cast.

The split looks like trivia until you count what it saves. Because the brain is pluggable, variety is data, not code: one brain class drives the basic chaser, the ranged goon that arrives with week 9's elites, and Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, alike — the bodies differ in stats, abilities, and price, and all of that lives in tuning files. This is precisely the property the director's spawn tables exploit: a table row is a body plus a cost, and the director buys bodies without ever caring what drives them. How the director converts greed into purchases → concept-ire.html#spawn-tables.

One more property, and it's the one with the invoice attached: the brain exists only on the server. Clients never run AI. They receive the goose's replicated movement the way they receive every other fact flowing down — the goose never sends a Server RPC, because the goose already lives where the answers are made. The consequence is that every brain ticks on the server's frame, which is why NPC count — not player count — is the knob that melts servers, and why the director carries a hard entity cap. Where the cap lives and why fewer, meaner geese win → concept-ire.html#entity-cap. What eighty brains cost a frame, in numbers → concept-perf.html#cost-model.

The carpet

A brain that wants to move needs an answer to an expensive question: where can feet go? Raw level geometry is the wrong place to ask — answering there means collision math against everything in the rift, per query, per goose. So the engine answers the question once, ahead of time: it bakes a NavMesh — a walkable carpet laid over the level geometry, simplified into polygons that mean "feet are legal here." Every MoveTo any brain ever issues becomes a path query against the carpet, not the world. That is the entire reason a hundred pathfinders per rift is even thinkable. One expensive bake, a million cheap reads.

The carpet is only woven inside a NavMeshBoundsVolume you place in the level. Placing it is a week-3 chore; forgetting it is the week-3 rite of passage: geese that refuse to MoveTo, path queries that return nothing, and a green debug overlay (press P in the editor) that stubbornly fails to appear. It's the bounds volume. It's always the bounds volume.

Then week 5 arrives, players start placing walls mid-session, and the baked carpet becomes a lie — so what does it cost to change the carpet at runtime? The engine offers two answers, priced very differently, chosen by the Runtime Generation setting on the nav mesh. Full dynamic regeneration keeps the carpet honest by re-baking it in pieces: any change re-rasterizes the affected tiles from raw geometry, at runtime, on the server. It exists for worlds whose ground truly changes shape — deforming terrain, floors that stop existing. Forty player-placed walls will eat the server with it, because every wall re-runs a slice of the bake during a siege.

Dynamic Modifiers Only is the answer walls want. The baked carpet stays baked, and obstacles carve holes in it: a wall registers as a nav obstacle, the engine subtracts that footprint from the walkable surface, and paths route around the hole. Carving is bookkeeping; re-rasterizing is geometry. A wall doesn't reshape the ground — it just forbids a patch of it — so pay for the hole, not for a new carpet. With the hole carved, the horde behaves correctly for free: paths bend around fresh construction, and when no path survives, week 5 widens goose targeting so the wall itself becomes the target and the gnawing begins. Where walls, placement, and the camp machine get built → week-05.html.

The flowchart brain

The body moves; something has to decide when. The slice's brains run on StateTree, Epic's newer AI model — a flowchart of states with enter conditions, tasks, and transitions, which maps onto how you already draw systems on whiteboards. Four words carry the whole model. A state is a named mode of being: Idle, Chase, Attack. An enter condition gates the door — the tree may only select a state whose condition currently passes. A task is what the state does while it is alive: stand around, follow a path, swing. A transition is the exit sign: when it fires, the current task stops and the tree re-selects from the root, again respecting every enter condition on the way down. Evaluators sit underneath, keeping the values the conditions read — "nearest target," "distance to it" — fresh each tick.

The figure draws week 3's entire brain: three boxes and four arrows. The root sits on top and re-selects on every transition, picking the topmost state whose enter condition passes. Idle enters when no target is sensed; its task is to stand around and honk on a timer. Chase enters when a target is sensed; its task is MoveTo(target) — a path query against the carpet. Attack enters when the target is within claw range; its task runs the claw ability. Two arrows carry the escalation — a player enters sense range, then comes within reach — and two carry the de-escalation: the target slips out of reach, or is lost entirely. Week 3 hands you this tree to write yourself, first-of-pattern → week-03.html.

Now walk one goose through one decision. It spawns; a brain possesses it; the root selects. Attack's enter condition fails — nothing in claw range. Chase's fails — nothing sensed. Idle passes, and the goose stands on the carpet, honking on its timer. A player walks into sense range: Idle's transition fires, the root re-selects, Chase's enter condition now passes, and Chase's task issues MoveTo. The carpet returns a path, and the goose's CharacterMovementComponent walks it. The player stops to harvest — greed has a posture — and the goose closes the gap. The reach transition fires, Attack selects, and its task activates the claw: a GameplayAbility, so the damage lands on the same ledger as every other transaction in the game, through the same ability shape whose first test swing landed on Gerald. The ability ends, the tree re-selects: still in reach means Attack again; a player scrambling away means Chase; a corpse means Idle. Why damage must ride the ledger → concept-gas.html#ledger. And note the goose owns an AbilitySystemComponent of its own — cheap to add at birth, and the reason week 9's elites can be handed storm resistances like any other transaction. Where an ASC lives, for players and for geese → concept-gas.html#asc-placement.

One honest caveat, with the retreat criteria written down in advance. StateTree is newer than Behavior Trees, and the older model has ten times the written material. Fall back to Behavior Trees on operational grounds only: two blocking problems in a row that community searches cannot answer, or a StateTree editor that keeps falling over under 5.7. Conceptual discomfort is not a criterion — an unfamiliar brain model at 9pm is learning, not evidence, and if a tooling fight grinds past the valve, that's what the valve is for → stuck.html. States, conditions, tasks, transitions transfer to whatever runs them. The model is the part worth learning.

The tap

The horde needs a source, and week 3's is deliberately dumb: a WaveSpawner — a server-side actor that, every interval, spawns N goons at a spawn point and hands each one a brain. That is the whole shape: a timer, a count, a spot on the carpet. It exists so the horde flows before anything intelligent decides the flow; in week 4 the director takes over the deciding, converting the expedition's own greed into wave budgets. Who funds the tap, and how the budget becomes bodies → concept-ire.html#economy.

One cost note, with a fence around it. Spawning is the most expensive moment of a goose's life — actor creation, possession, and opening a replication channel all land together — so if wave spawns visibly hitch the frame, pool: keep dead geese and re-dress them instead of destroying and spawning. Simple pooling, and only when a measured hitch says so. Don't gold-plate the tap.

The doorway

Here is the payoff that costs nothing: geese and players wear the same capsule collision, so body-blocking is free. Two capsules cannot occupy the same space, which means a player planted in a doorway means something — the horde cannot flow through the space a teammate refuses to yield, and holding ground becomes a verb nobody had to code. A body is terrain that moves.

The craft is in the tuning, and every knob is Tier-2 legal: capsule radius decides how many geese fit abreast in a funnel; mass and the CharacterMovementComponent's push interactions decide whether a charge can shove a defender out of the slot or whether the defender is a planted fact. Tune those until standing in the gap reads as a decision, and you have a tank role invented out of pure collision — no rigid bodies, no ragdolls, all legibility. Week 5 turns the trick into a game: walls carve the carpet into funnels, funnels concentrate the horde, and a body in the funnel's mouth is the cork. What Tier 2 permits and forbids → doctrine-physics.html#tier-test. Why pushing rides the CMC for free → doctrine-physics.html#cmc.

Eighty geese, one wall

Everything above scales politely until the geese have to share. Default path-following gives every goose its own path and zero awareness of colleagues: fifteen chasers read as enthusiasm; sixty to eighty converging on one wall line read as a mosh pit — capsules jostling for the same polygon, shoving each other off their paths, and re-pathing every time they're displaced, with the server paying for every re-path. The fix is one component swap and one dial. The swap: give the AIControllers a CrowdFollowingComponent, which replaces solo path-following with detour crowd avoidance — each agent steers around its neighbors while following its path, and the horde arrives as a flow instead of a scrum. The dial: avoidance quality, and it turns down — at eighty geese, low-quality avoidance is invisible in gameplay and visible on the server frame. Make both moves before buying anything else.

That sentence is week 8's opening move at the wall line, made under measurement — profile, change one thing, profile again. Where does the swap become a Friday number? → week-08.html. The six levers and their landmines → concept-perf.html#passes. And when avoidance is tuned and the frame still bleeds, the honest lever is fewer geese: the director's entity cap → concept-ire.html#entity-cap.
