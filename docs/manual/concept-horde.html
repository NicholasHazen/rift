Brains on the carpet

When to (re)read this: before week 3's first session — before the first goose gets a brain — then again when week 5's walls start editing the carpet, and again before week 8 sends eighty geese at one wall line.

An NPC (a non-player character — any creature the server drives) is three cheap parts in this game: a rented brain, a disposable body, and a pre-computed answer to the question "where can feet go?" This page assembles one goose from those parts: possession, the carpet, the flowchart. Then it scales the goose up: the tap that pours bodies, the doorway where a player becomes terrain, and the one component swap that keeps eighty geese from becoming a mosh pit. Everything here comes with its price tag attached, because the horde is the server's biggest bill.

The brain in the jar

Start with a sentence you already know from the player's side: a PlayerController possesses a Character — hands and eyes given a body. An NPC is the same sentence with the subject swapped: an AIController possesses a Pawn, the engine's word for any possessable body. The controller is a brain in a jar, pluggable into any body. It is not the goose; it is the thing that decides for the goose. The body carries the mesh, the collision capsule (the pill-shaped shell the world pushes against), and attributes like health, while the brain carries the decisions. Where the player half of this troupe lives, and which character owns which fact → concept-anatomy.html#cast.

The split looks like trivia until you count what it saves. Because the brain is pluggable, variety is data, not code: one brain class drives the basic chaser, the ranged goon that arrives with week 9's elites, and Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, alike. The bodies differ in stats, abilities, and price, and all of that lives in tuning files. This is precisely the property the director (the game's AI dungeon master) exploits with its spawn tables: a table row is a body plus a cost, and the director buys bodies without ever caring what drives them. How the director converts greed into purchases → concept-ire.html#spawn-tables.

One more property, and it's the one with the invoice attached: the brain exists only on the server. Clients never run AI. They receive the goose's replicated movement — position facts the server streams down — the way they receive every other fact in the game. The goose never sends a Server RPC (a client-to-server function call), because the goose already lives where the answers are made. The consequence is that every brain ticks on the server's frame, which is why NPC count — not player count — is the knob that melts servers, and why the director carries a hard entity cap. Where the cap lives and why fewer, meaner geese win → concept-ire.html#entity-cap. What eighty brains cost a frame, in numbers → concept-perf.html#cost-model.

The carpet

A brain that wants to move needs an answer to an expensive question: where can feet go? Raw level geometry is the wrong place to ask — answering there means collision math against everything in the rift, per query, per goose. So the engine answers the question once, ahead of time, by baking a NavMesh: a walkable carpet laid over the level geometry, simplified into polygons that mean "feet are legal here." Every MoveTo any brain ever issues — the basic "walk over there" order — becomes a path query against the carpet, not the world. That is the entire reason a hundred pathfinders per rift is even thinkable. One expensive bake, a million cheap reads.

The carpet is only woven inside a NavMeshBoundsVolume — an invisible box you drag into the level. Placing it is a week-3 chore, and forgetting it is the week-3 rite of passage: geese that refuse to MoveTo, path queries that return nothing, and a green debug overlay (press P in the editor) that stubbornly fails to appear. It's the bounds volume. It's always the bounds volume.

Then week 5 arrives, players start placing walls mid-session, and the baked carpet becomes a lie — so what does it cost to change the carpet at runtime? The engine offers two answers, priced very differently, and you choose between them with the Runtime Generation setting on the nav mesh. Full dynamic regeneration keeps the carpet honest by re-baking it in pieces: any change re-rasterizes the affected tiles from raw geometry, at runtime, on the server. It exists for worlds whose ground truly changes shape — deforming terrain, floors that stop existing. Forty player-placed walls will eat the server with it, because every wall re-runs a slice of the bake during a siege.

Dynamic Modifiers Only is the answer walls want. The baked carpet stays baked, and obstacles carve holes in it: a wall registers as a nav obstacle, the engine subtracts that footprint from the walkable surface, and paths route around the hole. A wall doesn't reshape the ground; it only forbids a patch of it. So you pay for the hole, not for a new carpet. Carving is bookkeeping; re-rasterizing is geometry.

With the hole carved, the horde behaves correctly for free, because paths bend around fresh construction on their own. And when no path survives at all, week 5 widens goose targeting so the wall itself becomes the target and the gnawing begins. Where walls, placement, and the camp machine get built → week-05.html.

The figure prices the two answers side by side, and both panels show the same event — a wall landing on the baked carpet. On the left, under full dynamic regeneration, the tiles around the wall light up, because each one re-runs a slice of the bake from raw geometry, at runtime, on the server, mid-siege. On the right, under Dynamic Modifiers Only, the carpet stays baked, the wall's footprint is subtracted as a hole, and the path bends around it. The same wall carries two prices: geometry on the left, bookkeeping on the right.

The flowchart brain

The body moves; something has to decide when. The slice (Stage 1's playable build) runs its brains on StateTree, Epic's newer AI model. A StateTree is a flowchart of states with enter conditions, tasks, and transitions, which means it maps onto how you already draw systems on whiteboards. Four words carry the whole model. A state is a named mode of being: Idle, Chase, Attack. An enter condition gates the door — the tree may only select a state whose condition currently passes. A task is what the state does while it is alive: stand around, follow a path, swing. A transition is the exit sign: when it fires, the current task stops and the tree re-selects from the root, again respecting every enter condition on the way down. Evaluators sit underneath, keeping the values the conditions read fresh each tick — values like "nearest target" and "distance to it."

The figure draws week 3's entire brain: three boxes and four arrows. The root sits on top and re-selects on every transition, picking the topmost state whose enter condition passes. Idle enters when no target is sensed, and its task is to stand around and honk on a timer. Chase enters when a target is sensed, and its task is MoveTo(target), a path query against the carpet. Attack enters when the target is within claw range, and its task runs the claw ability. Two arrows carry the escalation, as a player enters sense range and then comes within reach. Two more carry the de-escalation, when the target slips out of reach or is lost entirely. Week 3 hands you this tree to write yourself, because it is a first-of-pattern (the first instance of any pattern is built by hand) → week-03.html.

Now walk one goose through one decision. It spawns, a brain possesses it, and the root makes its first selection. Attack's enter condition fails, because nothing is in claw range, and Chase's fails too, because nothing is sensed. That leaves Idle, whose condition passes, so the goose stands on the carpet and honks on its timer. Then a player walks into sense range: Idle's transition fires, the root re-selects, Chase's enter condition now passes, and Chase's task issues MoveTo. The carpet returns a path, and the goose's CharacterMovementComponent (the engine's built-in character mover) walks it. The player stops to harvest, because greed has a posture, and the goose closes the gap. The reach transition fires, Attack selects, and its task activates the claw, which is a GameplayAbility (the engine's unit of activatable action). That means the damage lands on the same ledger as every other transaction in the game, through the same ability shape whose first test swing landed on Gerald. When the ability ends, the tree re-selects once more: still in reach means Attack, a player scrambling away means Chase, and a corpse means Idle. Why damage must ride the ledger → concept-gas.html#ledger. Note too that the goose owns an AbilitySystemComponent of its own, a component that carries a creature's abilities and attributes. It is cheap to add at birth, and it is the reason week 9's elites can be handed storm resistances like any other transaction. Where an ASC lives, for players and for geese → concept-gas.html#asc-placement.

One honest caveat remains, and it comes with the retreat criteria written down in advance. StateTree is newer than Behavior Trees, the older AI model, which has ten times the written material behind it. So fall back to Behavior Trees on operational grounds only: two blocking problems in a row that community searches cannot answer, or a StateTree editor that keeps falling over under 5.8. Conceptual discomfort is not a criterion, because an unfamiliar brain model at 9pm is learning, not evidence. And if a tooling fight grinds past the valve, that's what the valve is for → stuck.html. States, conditions, tasks, and transitions transfer to whatever runs them. The model is the part worth learning.

The tap

The horde needs a source, and week 3's is deliberately dumb. It is a WaveSpawner, a server-side actor that spawns N goons at a spawn point every interval and hands each one a brain. That is the whole shape: a timer, a count, a spot on the carpet. It exists so the horde flows before anything intelligent decides the flow, and in week 4 the director takes over the deciding, converting the expedition's own greed into wave budgets. Who funds the tap, and how the budget becomes bodies → concept-ire.html#economy.

One cost note comes with a fence around it. Spawning is the most expensive moment of a goose's life, because actor creation, possession, and opening a replication channel all land together. So if wave spawns visibly hitch the frame, pool them: keep dead geese and re-dress them instead of destroying and spawning. Pool simply, though, and only when a measured hitch says so. Don't gold-plate the tap.

The doorway

Here is the payoff that costs nothing: geese and players wear the same capsule collision, so body-blocking is free. Two capsules cannot occupy the same space, which means a player planted in a doorway means something — the horde cannot flow through the space a teammate refuses to yield, and holding ground becomes a verb nobody had to code. A body is terrain that moves.

The craft is in the tuning, and every knob stays inside Tier 2, the game's cheap-collision physics budget. Capsule radius decides how many geese fit abreast in a funnel. Mass, together with the CharacterMovementComponent's push interactions, decides whether a charge can shove a defender out of the slot, or whether the defender is a planted fact. Tune those until standing in the gap reads as a decision, and you have a tank role invented out of pure collision — no rigid bodies, no ragdolls, all legibility. Week 5 turns the trick into a game: walls carve the carpet into funnels, funnels concentrate the horde, and a body in the funnel's mouth is the cork. What Tier 2 permits and forbids → doctrine-physics.html#tier-test. Why pushing rides the CMC for free → doctrine-physics.html#cmc.

Eighty geese, one wall

Everything above scales politely until the geese have to share. Default path-following gives every goose its own path and zero awareness of its colleagues. Fifteen chasers read as enthusiasm, but sixty to eighty converging on one wall line read as a mosh pit: the capsules jostle for the same polygon, shove each other off their paths, and re-path every time they are displaced. The server pays for every re-path. The fix is one component swap and one dial. The swap is to give the AIControllers a CrowdFollowingComponent, which replaces solo path-following with detour crowd avoidance. Each agent then steers around its neighbors while following its path, so the horde arrives as a flow instead of a scrum. The dial is avoidance quality, and it turns down, not up. At eighty geese, low-quality avoidance is invisible in gameplay but plainly visible on the server frame. Make both moves before buying anything else.

That sentence is week 8's opening move at the wall line, made under measurement — profile, change one thing, profile again. Where does the swap become a Friday number? → week-08.html. The six levers and their landmines → concept-perf.html#passes. And when avoidance is tuned and the frame still bleeds, the honest lever is fewer geese: the director's entity cap → concept-ire.html#entity-cap.
