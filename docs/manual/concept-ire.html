Concept · Ire & the director

A dungeon master with a wallet

When to (re)read this: once, before week 4's first session — before you hand-write UIreSubsystem — and again whenever the waves feel wrong: flat while the expedition gorges, or toothless when the pressure should be biting.

Ire is currency players mint by being greedy — every harvest tick, every kill, every structure standing — and the director spends that currency on violence: bigger waves, faster waves, nastier compositions. Difficulty stops being a setting and becomes a consequence. This page holds the signature system whole — the economy, its Unreal shapes, the spawn table, the cap, and the lineage it steals from — so the weeks that touch it can link here instead of re-deriving it.

The economy

You already know the dungeon master: the server, keeper of the one true world, validator of every request. (Who decides, and why can't clients? → concept-netcode.html#authority.) This page hands the DM a wallet. Every greedy act the expedition takes drops coins in, and the DM spends those coins on exactly one product: violence, delivered to your camp. A cautious expedition gets a quiet rift. A greedy one summons hell. The dial sits in the players' hands every minute of every session.

Greed mints ire. The pool funds the director. The director spends it on waves. The waves press back on the greedy. The figure below draws that sentence as four boxes in a row. Greed sits on the far left — harvest, build, kill — drawn in ember, because minting is the players' clock. An ember arrow feeds the ire pool, the one number the whole expedition shares, labeled GameState because that is where clients read it. A second ember arrow feeds the director, and there the palette flips: the director and its waves are drawn in storm, because from the pool onward it is the world answering. The line beneath the row closes the loop — pressure feeds back on the greedy. Difficulty as consequence, not setting.

One greedy evening, traced

Run an evening through the loop. The expedition lands and plays it careful: a handful of harvest ticks, no camp yet, two wandering geese put down. The pool collects pocket change; the director wakes on schedule, converts it, and can afford a couple of goons — the quiet rift, working as designed. Then somebody places an extractor. It harvests on a timer, which means it mints on a timer, and the walls that go up to protect it mint by the minute just by standing there. The pool fattens while everyone's hands are busy. A few wakes later the director is buying real waves — and here the loop shows its teeth, because the kills that defend the camp are themselves mint events. Fighting the answer is also greed. The expedition that wants a quieter rift has exactly one honest move: take a hand off the dial.

The mint is testable to the exact coin, and that is its correct first use: set ire-per-kill to a number you'll recognize, put Gerald down, and watch the pool jump by exactly that much. The economy's first unit test is a training dummy.

The precise loop

Now the precise version. The mint: three families of event feed the pool — harvest ticks, kills, and structure-minutes — each worth a tuned amount of ire. The pool: one number for the entire expedition. Ire pools expedition-wide, which makes greed a social fact — "WHO built a fourth extractor?" is a feature. The spend: every N seconds the director wakes, converts the accrued pool into a wave budget along a tuning curve, and shops the spawn table until the budget is gone. Three verbs — mint, pool, spend — and the whole machine is described. The loop keeps one end deliberately open — whether the pool also drains when the expedition lays low — and that question lives in the register with a named default, linked from the question box below. The rift answers exactly as loudly as it is asked.

The Unreal shapes

The pool needs a home, and Unreal has a shape made for it: a World Subsystem — an engine-managed, world-scoped singleton. A service container, in other words; a shape you know in your sleep. UIreSubsystem is that container, and it runs on the server only: the accrual functions the mint events call, the pool itself, the tier math. It is the DM's private arithmetic, and clients have no business reading the DM's notes directly.

The subsystem being server-only leaves one fact stranded: every HUD in the expedition needs to show how angry the rift is, and a client cannot ask a service it doesn't run. So the current ire value is mirrored onto GameState as an ordinary replicated property — the pool is nobody's private property, it is one fact the whole table shares — and clients react to the mirror changing the way they react to any fact arriving. The subsystem is the math; the GameState mirror is the fact. Which character owns which kind of fact, and why GameState rather than PlayerState? → concept-anatomy.html#cast.

The spender is a DirectorComponent, and its loop is deliberately dumb: wake every N seconds; convert whatever the pool accrued into a wave budget; then shop — filter the spawn table down to the rows the current ire tier admits, weighted-pick one, pay its cost, spawn it, and repeat until the budget is spent or the cap says stop. It lives where the subsystem lives, server-side. No client ever sees the director — only its purchases, arriving at the wall.

Every number this system runs on — ire per harvest tick, per kill, per structure-minute, the wake interval, the budget curve, every row's cost and weight, the cap itself — lives in JSON, never in a constant. That is rule 6 at full power: from the week this system exists, agents author game balance as text files, and retuning is a file edit, not a recompile. How does a JSON edit become live tuning? → concept-anatomy.html#tuning-as-text. The whole machine is also drivable by hand — gift ire, force a wave, summon an elite — which is what makes tuning nights and live-DM playtests possible at all. What can the Remote do today? → fun-engines.html#remote.

The spawn table

The table the director shops from is content, not code — a JSON document the fleet authors and you review like a schema, because it is one. A row is four facts: what to spawn (a body plus a brain), what it costs the director, how likely it is to be picked (its weight), and which ire tier admits it. The tier column is how composition scales with greed: cheap goons live on the bottom rows, brutes and titled elites unlock as the expedition climbs — so a rich director doesn't just buy more, it buys worse. When week 9's rare window arrives, it rides this same schema, with elite rows additionally gated on the world's clock.

The sketch below is the shape of the whole tuning file, spawn table included. The numbers are placeholders; the schema is the contract.

{
  "irePerHarvestTick": 1,
  "irePerKill": 5,
  "irePerStructureMinute": 2,
  "director": {
    "wakeIntervalSeconds": 20,
    "entityCap": 80,
    "spawnTable": [
      { "type": "Goon",     "cost": 10,  "weight": 60, "minIreTier": 0 },
      { "type": "Sprinter", "cost": 25,  "weight": 25, "minIreTier": 1 },
      { "type": "Brute",    "cost": 60,  "weight": 12, "minIreTier": 2 },
      { "type": "Elite",    "cost": 150, "weight": 3,  "minIreTier": 3 }
    ]
  }
}

One property makes rows this cheap: an NPC's brain is pluggable into any body, so a new enemy variant is a data entry, not an engineering project. Why are brains pluggable? → concept-horde.html#brains.

The cap

The director carries a hard entity cap — a ceiling on living NPCs that no budget may exceed, written into the JSON like everything else. NPC count is this project's primary performance knob, and at siege time it is the honest lever; the cap is the point where the perf discipline and the design agree. (What does one goon actually cost the server at 24 players? → concept-perf.html#cost-model.) Past the cap, escalation is composition, not count: a richer budget shops the meaner rows instead of buying a bigger crowd. Treat the cap as a design fact rather than a concession — fewer, meaner enemies is usually better gameplay anyway.

The lineage

Here is the problem every director in the lineage exists to solve: a spender with no sense of pacing produces a flat drone. Pressure arriving at a constant rate reads as noise, and noise is neither dread nor relief. Left 4 Dead's AI director is the canonical answer — build-up, peak, relax: it watches intensity and deliberately relents, because the relax phase is what makes the peaks legible. Deep Rock Galactic paces its swarms the same way and announces them, selling dread on a schedule. Risk of Rain winds difficulty from time alone — a pure world's-clock design.

Steal the skeleton; keep the twist. In all three of those games, the machine mints its own budget from time and intensity. Here, the players mint it — the pacing shape is borrowed, the authorship is not. Before writing the spend loop, read how the canonical director decides when to spawn and when to relent, and which build-up / peak / relax knobs Valve exposed → shelf.html#ai (the Left 4 Dead director deck). The pacing skeleton is proven; the mint is yours.

One stream, two customers

Notice what the mint events already are: a running log of everything notable the expedition did — harvested, built, killed, died, hauled a rock through the portal. Log them once, as one schema with two customers: the ire subsystem tallies them live to price the violence, and the Chronicle reads them back at debrief to tell the story. You own that schema — it is a schema-design problem, which is home turf. Why must story events ride an event log instead of being reconstructed from replication? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments. What does the log become at debrief? → fun-engines.html#chronicle.

And ire is only half the pressure. Ire is the players' clock — it moves when they act, and only then. Instability is the world's clock — it rises on its own, however saintly the expedition plays. The two converge on the exit window, where the whole game concentrates into one decision. How do two clocks make one decision? → the-slice.html#two-clocks.
