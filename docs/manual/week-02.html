Teaching the world to fight (GAS)

Week 02Phase: Core loopBudget: 6 sessions, one pre-spentresolution: full
Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — Gerald absorbed 412 recorded blows and one orbital launch. Awarded: Employee of the Rift. Gerald did not attend.

By Friday you will have two clients trading abilities and fighting Gerald across a dedicated server (a standalone server process no player hosts), and you will understand why no number in this game ever gets edited directly again. This is the steepest week of the twelve, with the worst effort-to-visible-progress ratio in the plan, so it is planned like a climb: six sessions, one of them deliberately empty.

▣ Gate — definition of done

Two clients exchange abilities and fight Gerald in PIE (Play In Editor — running the game inside the editor) with the dedicated-server option. Health replicates, meaning the server's number reaches every client. Death and respawn survive 100 ms simulated lag, and every hit makes a sound and a number. The gate is functional, not feel — feel is week 10's job, and PvP feel is Stage 2's.

The rift needs combat, and an RPG's combat means abilities: attacks, dodges, storm damage-over-time, and elite specials. Eventually, per your standing belief in procedural generation, it also means generated combinations of all of the above. You could hand-roll damage in a day. You're not going to, and the reason is worth stating straight, because mid-week you will be tempted to bail. The Gameplay Ability System — GAS, the engine's framework for networked abilities — is over-engineered for week 2 and correctly-engineered for week 40. It ships networked prediction (clients acting before the server confirms), cooldowns, costs, stacking, and status effects as configuration. Rolling your own instead means re-solving networked ability prediction — one of the genre's genuinely hard problems — by hand, later, under pressure. Pay the tax now, while the codebase is small and forgiving.

One piece of honesty belongs up front, because it protects the gate's wording. GAS prediction covers your own ability activation, your own costs and cooldowns, and the effects you apply to yourself, but it does not predict the damage you deal to anyone else. Damage to Gerald therefore arrives a round-trip later, and that latency is normal, not a bug. The honest fix is local activation feedback from the JuiceKit, the project's game-feel component, which fires the moment you act rather than the moment the server agrees. What exactly gets predicted, what round-trips, and why is the boundary drawn there? → concept-gas.html#prediction-scope

The trap this week: mistaking that round-trip for brokenness. You'll know you're in it when you're tuning hit-feel in a duel instead of hitting Gerald. The move: believe the gate, which asks for functional rather than feel, and do not burn a valve session — the 90-minute stuck escape — chasing PvP hit-feel that stock GAS cannot deliver. Combat identity is a Stage-2 question, and the register, the manual's page of standing decisions, is already holding it.

The sessions

The week runs six sessions. S1 through S5 are the climb, while S6 is deliberately empty, and the reason it exists is written just below. Done-marks land as sessions close, per the week-0 convention.

S1 — Socket

[NICK — by hand] Enable the GameplayAbilities plugin, then make the placement decision once, while it still costs nothing to make. Players get the AbilitySystemComponent — the ASC, the component that runs a character's abilities — on the PlayerState. Because the PlayerState is the per-player object that outlives any one pawn, their attributes survive respawn. While you are in there, bump the PlayerState's NetUpdateFrequency to around 100, because its default is far too lazy for combat state.

NPCs get their ASC on the Pawn instead, since a disposable body can carry disposable state. Crib the initialization order shamelessly from Narxim-GAS-Example, a durable and maintained repo that shows both placements, because the order is famously fiddly and this is not where your learning budget goes. Where should the ASC live, and what breaks on respawn if you choose wrong? → concept-gas.html#asc-placement.

Done when: InitAbilityActorInfo has provably run on the server and on each client. A log line, a breakpoint, or showdebug abilitysystem showing the ASC live on a client's screen — any one of them proves it. The gotcha box below is what happens when it hasn't.

[FLEET] S1 touches Build.cs with new module dependencies, so the fleet refreshes compile_commands.json the same evening — the week-0 rule about stale databases applies from the first header.

S2 — Ledger

[NICK — by hand] This session builds the AttributeSet, which holds a character's numbers like Health and Stamina, plus one damage GameplayEffect and Gerald, the target dummy that absorbs the transactions. A GameplayEffect is the package a change to those numbers travels in. Two rules live inside the set itself: it clamps values in PreAttributeChange and detects death in PostGameplayEffectExecute. Rules belong with the numbers they govern, not scattered across the abilities that touch them. Why is every damage number a transaction on a ledger? → concept-gas.html#ledger. Done when: a server-applied effect moves Gerald's Health, both clients watch the same number change, and Gerald has his nameplate. He is born this session, and the personality ships with the body.

S3 — First verb

[NICK — by hand] The first verb is the melee ability, and its shape is a short chain: it activates, traces for a target, then applies the GameplayEffect. This is the project's first GameplayAbility, an activatable action the engine runs and networks for you. First-of-pattern work is yours, so do not delegate this one. Wire it into the JuiceKit so every hit lands with a sound and a floating damage number, plus Gerald's scream, the one free SFX his contract guarantees. Done when: both clients see the same damage number float off Gerald, and the hit screams. LitRPG appetite: fed.

S4 — Second verb

[NICK — by hand] The second verb is the ranged ability. It fires a projectile rather than a hitscan, the instant-hit trace, because a projectile travels over time and so teaches more replication. Cooldown and stamina cost both ride GameplayEffects, so you touch the cost-and-cooldown machinery exactly once: at Commit, the point in the lifecycle where they get checked and paid. Where do cost and cooldown actually get paid? → concept-gas.html#lifecycle. Whether stamina stays in the slice at all is the register's question, not this session's. Tonight it exists only so the machinery gets learned. Done when: the projectile fires on a client that isn't the server, costs Stamina, and refuses to fire during its cooldown.

[FLEET] After S3 the first ability exists, so the pattern is open for replication, which means the fleet may scaffold the projectile from the melee ability's shape. The cost-and-cooldown wiring stays in your hands.

S5 — Death & Yeet

[NICK — by hand] The death chain runs from Health reaching zero to death, then ragdoll, then respawn. Because the ASC lives on the PlayerState, that whole trip loses no state, which is S1's placement decision paying out. Then build Yeet. Done when: a player dies and respawns cleanly under 100 ms of simulated lag, which PIE's network emulation supplies, and Clip #003 exists. Clip #003 is obviously Gerald in orbit.

S6 — Buffer

GAS always overruns. This session is pre-spent so the calendar doesn't panic. The trough is budgeted, which is what keeps a low-dopamine week from reading as a failed one. This is the pre-spent-buffer pattern, and week 2 is its home. Any future week built around a system famous for eating an extra session copies it from here. Done when: the gate is green, whether this session got used or not. An overrun you already paid for is a plan.

⚑ Fun payload — Yeet, and Gerald

Player: Yeet is a shove with comically overtuned knockback that launches ragdolls into low orbit. Physics comedy is free, and Yeet is sneakily your first knockback GameplayEffect, which sieges will reuse forever. Per the physics doctrine it graduates from gag to first-class verb: shoving the horde off a chunk, off a wall line, or into a hazard is legitimate tactics. It rides the CharacterMovementComponent — the CMC, the engine's built-in character mover — and its networked prediction is already paid for, so Yeet costs only Tier-2 prices. Builder: the target dummy is named Gerald, he has a nameplate, and he screams (one free SFX) when hit. You will spend dozens of hours with Gerald, so he deserves a personality. His career starts this week, and his canon lives in the bestiary.

What this week teaches

Five ideas run this week, and one page owns all of them. The AbilitySystemComponent is the socket that makes a character a citizen of the rules engine — and where it lives is a real decision, made in S1.

Attributes are numbers with rules, living in an AttributeSet that clamps and judges its own values.

GameplayEffects are the only legal way attributes change — signed ledger transactions, and nobody edits the account balance directly.

GameplayTags are hierarchical adjectives (State.Stunned, Damage.Type.Storm) the whole system can query — the shared vocabulary the director and the storm will interrogate from week 9 on.

And abilities have a lifecycle, from CanActivate through Commit to the work and the End — the machinery S4 touches once and then reuses forever. How do the five ideas fit into one told story, with Gerald as the worked example? → concept-gas.html
