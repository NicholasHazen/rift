Teaching the world to fight (GAS)

Week 02Phase: Core loopBudget: 6 sessions, one pre-spentresolution: full
Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — Gerald absorbed 412 recorded blows and one orbital launch. Awarded: Employee of the Rift. Gerald did not attend.

By Friday you will have two clients trading abilities and fighting Gerald across a dedicated server, and you will understand why no number in this game ever gets edited directly again. This is the steepest week of the twelve — the worst effort-to-visible-progress ratio in the plan — so it is planned like a climb: six sessions, one of them deliberately empty.

▣ Gate — definition of done

Two clients exchange abilities and fight Gerald in PIE with the dedicated-server option; health replicates; death and respawn survive 100 ms simulated lag; every hit makes a sound and a number. The gate is functional, not feel — feel is week 10's job, and PvP feel is Stage 2's.

The rift needs combat, and an RPG's combat means abilities — attacks, dodges, storm damage-over-time, elite specials, and eventually (per your proc-gen belief) generated combinations of all of the above. You could hand-roll damage in a day. You're not going to, and here's the straight version of why, because mid-week you will be tempted to bail: the Gameplay Ability System is over-engineered for week 2 and correctly-engineered for week 40. It ships networked prediction, cooldowns, costs, stacking, and status effects as configuration. Rolling your own means re-solving networked ability prediction — one of the genre's genuinely hard problems — by hand, later, under pressure. Pay the tax now, while the codebase is small and forgiving.

One piece of honesty before the first swing, because it protects the gate's wording: GAS prediction covers your own ability activation, your own costs and cooldowns, and effects you apply to yourself — it does not predict the damage you deal to anyone else. Damage to Gerald arrives a round-trip later, and that latency is normal, not a bug; the JuiceKit's local activation feedback is the honest fix. What exactly gets predicted, what round-trips, and why is the boundary drawn there? → concept-gas.html#prediction-scope

The trap this week: mistaking that round-trip for brokenness. You'll know you're in it when you're tuning hit-feel in a duel instead of hitting Gerald. The move: believe the gate — functional, not feel — and do not burn a valve session chasing PvP hit-feel that stock GAS cannot deliver; combat identity is a Stage-2 question and the register is already holding it.

The sessions

Six sessions. S1 through S5 are the climb; S6 is deliberately empty, and the reason it exists is written under it. Done-marks land as sessions close, per the week-0 convention.

S1 — Socket

[NICK — by hand] Enable the GameplayAbilities plugin, then make the placement decision once, while it costs nothing to make: players get the AbilitySystemComponent on the PlayerState, so attributes survive respawn — and bump the PlayerState's NetUpdateFrequency to around 100, because its default is far too lazy for combat state. NPCs get theirs on the Pawn: disposable body, disposable state. Crib the initialization order shamelessly from Narxim-GAS-Example — a durable, maintained repo that shows both placements; the order is famously fiddly and this is not where your learning budget goes. Where should the ASC live, and what breaks on respawn if you choose wrong? → concept-gas.html#asc-placement. Done when: InitAbilityActorInfo has provably run on the server and on each client — log line or breakpoint, your pick, or showdebug abilitysystem showing the ASC live on a client's screen — because the gotcha box below is what happens when it hasn't.

[FLEET] S1 touches Build.cs with new module dependencies, so the fleet refreshes compile_commands.json the same evening — the week-0 rule about stale databases applies from the first header.

S2 — Ledger

[NICK — by hand] The AttributeSet (Health, Stamina), one damage GameplayEffect, and Gerald the target dummy to absorb the transactions. Two rules live inside the set itself: clamp values in PreAttributeChange, and detect death in PostGameplayEffectExecute — rules belong with the numbers they govern, not scattered across abilities. Why is every damage number a transaction on a ledger? → concept-gas.html#ledger. Done when: a server-applied effect moves Gerald's Health, both clients watch the same number change, and Gerald has his nameplate — he's born this session; the personality ships with the body.

S3 — First verb

[NICK — by hand] The melee ability: activate → trace → apply the GameplayEffect. This is the project's first GameplayAbility, and first-of-pattern work is yours — no delegating this one. Wire it into the JuiceKit: hit sound, floating damage number — and Gerald's scream, the one free SFX his contract guarantees. Done when: both clients see the same damage number float off Gerald, and the hit screams. LitRPG appetite: fed.

S4 — Second verb

[NICK — by hand] The ranged ability — projectile, not hitscan, because a projectile teaches more replication. Cooldown and stamina cost ride GameplayEffects, so you touch the cost/cooldown machinery exactly once — at Commit, which is where the lifecycle checks and pays them. Where do cost and cooldown actually get paid? → concept-gas.html#lifecycle. Whether stamina stays in the slice at all is the register's question, not this session's; tonight it exists so the machinery gets learned. Done when: the projectile fires on a client that isn't the server, costs Stamina, and refuses to fire during its cooldown.

[FLEET] After S3 the first ability exists, so the pattern is open for replication: the fleet may scaffold the projectile from the melee ability's shape. The cost/cooldown wiring stays in your hands.

S5 — Death & Yeet

[NICK — by hand] Health reaches zero → death → ragdoll → respawn — and because the ASC lives on the PlayerState, the trip loses no state: S1's placement decision, paying out. Then build Yeet. Done when: a player dies and respawns cleanly under 100 ms simulated lag — PIE's network emulation supplies the lag — and Clip #003 exists, which is obviously Gerald in orbit.

S6 — Buffer

GAS always overruns. This session is pre-spent so the calendar doesn't panic: the trough is budgeted, which is what keeps a low-dopamine week from reading as a failed one. This is the pre-spent-buffer pattern, and week 2 is its home — any future week built around a system famous for eating an extra session copies it from here. Done when: the gate is green, whether this session got used or not. An overrun you already paid for is a plan.

⚑ Fun payload — Yeet, and Gerald

Player: Yeet — a shove with comically overtuned knockback that launches ragdolls into low orbit. Physics comedy is free, and Yeet is sneakily your first knockback GameplayEffect, which sieges will reuse forever. Per the physics doctrine it graduates from gag to first-class verb: shoving the horde off a chunk, off a wall line, or into a hazard is legitimate tactics, and because it rides the CharacterMovementComponent's existing networked prediction, it costs Tier-2 prices. Builder: the target dummy is named Gerald, Gerald has a nameplate, and Gerald screams (one free SFX) when hit. You will spend dozens of hours with Gerald; he deserves a personality. His career starts this week, and his canon lives in the bestiary.

What this week teaches

Five ideas run this week, and one page owns all of them. The AbilitySystemComponent is the socket that makes a character a citizen of the rules engine — and where it lives is a real decision, made in S1. Attributes are numbers with rules, living in an AttributeSet that clamps and judges its own values. GameplayEffects are the only legal way attributes change — signed ledger transactions, and nobody edits the account balance directly. GameplayTags are hierarchical adjectives (State.Stunned, Damage.Type.Storm) the whole system can query — the shared vocabulary the director and the storm will interrogate from week 9 on. And abilities have a lifecycle, from CanActivate through Commit to the work and the End — the machinery S4 touches once and then reuses forever.
