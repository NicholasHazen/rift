The Fun Mandate

Fun is a weekly deliverable, not a milestone. If a week ships working code and zero joy, the week failed — for the players who will eventually inherit that system, and more urgently for you, because momentum is this project's fuel, and joy is what refills the tank.

Where fun goes to wait

Here's the honest problem: graybox development, which means building the whole game in untextured placeholder shapes, is where fun goes to wait. Capsules sliding around a gray plane doing correct, joyless things — for months — is the standard indie path, and the abandonable one. The fix is not to abandon graybox, because art-before-fun is procrastination wearing a render pipeline. That is exactly why gray-box-until-week-10 is standing law, and why charm has to come from somewhere other than meshes.

The fix is to notice that fun is mostly free if you spend cleverness instead of art budget. Sound is free, physics comedy is free, and an absurd placeholder identity costs only a word list. Text is free too, and you read LitRPG, the stat-block fiction genre, so you already know what escalating system text does to a reader. That is why this doctrine institutionalizes the cheap stuff: four permanent engines, one committed bit, and a contract that puts joy on every week's invoice.

The four fun engines

There are four small, permanent systems, unlocked across the first six weeks, and each one is a machine that keeps producing fun for the rest of the project. On the index's week map they are the four ember markers — player-clock, every one, because each exists so you have something to push. Treat their build-out as seriously as the netcode. This page states what each engine is and why it earns permanent status. The machinery — hook inventories, command lists, word lists, the voice bible — accumulates on fun-engines.html, and the clips themselves live on clips.html.

The JuiceKit, unlocked week 1, is the rule that nothing ships numb. It is one tiny reusable feedback component — floating LitRPG combat text, sound stingers, particle pops, hitstop hooks — and every new mechanic plugs into it the day it is born, so nothing you build is ever silent, not even a graybox node taking its first hit. Which hooks exist, and what plugs in where? → fun-engines.html#juicekit

The Director's Remote, unlocked week 4, is a god panel: grant ire (the currency greed mints), force a wave, spawn an elite, kill all, rain ducks. It is test tooling that happens to be the most fun object in the project, and it graduates: first you play dungeon master over your own empty world, and by the friend-test nights you are doing it live, over your friends, mid-run. Which commands exist, and which week did each arrive? → fun-engines.html#remote

The Chronicle, unlocked week 6, is the end-of-run recap generated from the event log and written like a LitRPG chapter: who got greedy, who died richest, who carried. Version zero is string templates over real events. Later, local models on the Spark, the project's own compute box, polish the same log into prose. That quietly makes the Chronicle the embryo of the LLM content pipeline. Where do the event taxonomy and the voice bible live? → fun-engines.html#chronicle

The Friday Clip, unlocked week 0, is twenty seconds of this week's build, recorded every Friday and posted to the friends' Discord. It is accountability, a dev-log archive, and — by week 12 — a trailer's worth of raw material. Non-negotiable, even in ugly weeks. Especially in ugly weeks. Where does the reel accumulate, newest first? → clips.html

Commit to a bit

The second half of the mandate: graybox needs a personality, and absurdity is the cheapest personality on the market. Your enemies do not have art, so they could be gray capsules — or they could be, say, geese. A honk sound is free, and a goose named by a generator — Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm — costs one word list the fleet writes in five minutes. The moment the horde has a dumb, committed identity, every playtest becomes quotable, every clip becomes shareable, and the game acquires charm months before it acquires art.

The Bit itself is disposable, because real creature design arrives in Stage 2. But the charm infrastructure underneath it — the name generators, the title generators, the superlatives, the Chronicle's voice — survives forever. That is why the identity commits by Friday of week 3 and then stays committed: the engines above all feed on it, and a horde that changes its name weekly feeds nothing. The payload contract

Doctrine without an enforcement surface is a mood, so the mandate ships as a contract. Every week page carries a fun payload box, and it names two things: one for the player, whose build gets juicier, and one for the builder, who gets a toy. These payloads are scoped to hours, not days. They are scope, not garnish, so when a week runs long, the stretch goals get cut and the payload doesn't. Where does the weekly close execute the contract? → doctrine-os.html#friday

A payload that slips anyway rolls forward into the next week's box — it never silently dies. Where do rolled payloads get tracked? → fun-engines.html#fun-debt

Every week page carries a Fun Payload block: one thing for the player (the build gets juicier) and one for the builder (you get a toy). Payloads are scoped to hours, not days. If a week's payload slips, it rolls to the next week's — it never silently dies. Fun debt is tracked like tech debt.
Mentor's aside — There's a deeper reason this doctrine works, and it comes from a belief you hold: a game with compelling, social gameplay doesn't need to deliver everything on day one. The corollary is that charm scales down further than content does. RuneScape was charming before it was big, and Deep Rock was charming in gray caves. Charm-per-dollar is the one metric where a solo dev with a sense of humor beats a 200-person studio. Spend there.
