The rift pushes back

Week 03Phase: Core loopBudget: 5 sessionsDecision due: the Bitresolution: full
Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — The rift grew an immune system this week. Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, led fourteen others over the ridge and into the first line of the kill feed.

Fifteen geese crest the ridge in a wedge, honking in unison, the pitch bending as the charge closes. You plant yourself in a gray-box doorway and the wedge piles up against your capsule — a tank role nobody coded. The kill feed posts its first superlative. Two clients, one dedicated server, a hundred milliseconds of simulated lag, and nothing stutters. Clip #004 is already rolling.

By Friday you will have fifteen NPCs (non-player characters, the rift's own wildlife) chasing and biting two clients under a dedicated server, a standalone headless copy of the game, with simulated lag on the wire. Along the way you will understand why a horde costs so little, because the whole trick is three cheap parts: a brain in a jar, a walkable carpet, and a three-state flowchart. You will also have written down the project's first performance number, the baseline every later measurement argues against.

▣ Gate — definition of done

Fifteen NPCs chase and melee two clients without visible stutter, in PIE (Play In Editor — running the game inside the editor) with the dedicated-server checkbox, under net emulation — the simulated lag and loss that rule 5 keeps on permanently from this week. The baseline server frame time is committed to docs/perf-baseline.md. The horde has a name, a noise, and a kill feed with jokes in it. Why is the convenient topology the one that lies? → concept-netcode.html#topologies

Three weeks in, the world has value in it (nodes) and violence in it (abilities). Now the rift gets its immune system — the creatures that will answer the expedition's greed starting next week. This week they're dumb chasers, and that's correct. The director makes them mean later; this week only has to make them exist, cheaply and reliably. One law rides along: the horde is melee-only until week 9's ranged elite. Ranged pressure reshapes camp design, so it arrives with the elites, not before.

One prerequisite listen carries over from the week-1 pattern: before S1, hear the brains-on-the-carpet page through once, start to finish. It is a listen, not a session — the commute or the dishes carry it. It assembles one goose from a rented brain, a disposable body, and a pre-baked carpet, with every price tag attached, and this week is its lab.

The trap this week: making the horde smart. You'll know you're in it when the StateTree (the engine's visual state-machine) grows a fourth state before fifteen goons have chased anyone. The move: dumb chasers, en masse — menace is the director's job, and the director arrives next week.

The sessions

Five sessions. The first four build one goon and a tap that pours more of them; the fifth writes down what they cost. Each carries its done-when, per the week-0 convention; done-marks land as sessions close.

S1 — Body & carpet

[NICK] Four pieces make one goon that walks: an NPC pawn for a body, an AIController (the AI brain that drives a pawn) to possess it, a NavMesh bounds volume drawn over the gray-box, and a MoveTo aimed at the nearest player. The NavMesh bounds volume is simply the box that tells the engine where to bake its walkable surface. That is the entire session. Spawn the goon on the server, because it is world state, and clients meet it by replication the same way they meet a rock. If the NavMesh won't build, read the gotcha box before you read anything else. Done when: one goon walks to the nearest player in PIE with two clients and the dedicated-server checkbox.

S2 — Brain

[NICK — by hand] Hand-write your first StateTree yourself, because rule 4 says the first of a pattern belongs to you. Its three states are Idle, Chase, and Attack, and three is where it stays: the tree earns a fourth state when a playtest demands one, not before. Done when: the goon idles until a player enters range, chases, and swings on contact, and you can narrate every transition from the tree you drew.

[FLEET] The moment your tree stands, the pattern is replicable, so the fleet builds a second NPC variant from it, body and brain, ready for S4's spawner to pour.

S3 — Claws

[NICK] The attack routes through your week-2 GameplayAbility, the ability object that already deals your damage. That means every point of goose damage lands on the same ledger as player damage — one pipeline, not two. Better still, the sound-and-number feedback you wired in week 2 fires for goose bites for free. NPCs get an ASC of their own, the AbilitySystemComponent that holds an actor's stats and abilities. It runs in Minimal replication mode, which is cheap now and turns essential in week 9 when elites need storm resistances. Point the first bite at Gerald — the ledger already knows how to hurt him. Where does an NPC's ASC live, and what does Minimal mode skip? → concept-gas.html#asc-placement. Done when: a goose bite moves a player's health through the week-2 pipeline, and the hit makes a sound and a number like every other hit in the game.

S4 — The tap

[FLEET] A WaveSpawner drops N goons per interval from a spawn point. By week 3 this is boilerplate from your established patterns, so the fleet drafts it. You review it like you own it, because you do. Here is the one-line authority read: the spawner is server-only. The goons it spawns replicate, but the spawner itself never needs to. Done when: waves arrive on an interval in PIE + dedicated.

[NICK] If spawning hitches, add simple pooling — reuse goons instead of respawning fresh ones. But don't gold-plate the tap, because the director takes it over next week. What does a spawn actually cost, and when does pooling earn its keep? → concept-horde.html#spawner. Done when: spawning no longer hitches, or never did.

S5 — Credit & baseline

[NICK — by hand] Add per-player kill credit on PlayerState, the per-player record every client can read. It is quietly the embryo of contribution tracking, the thing that keeps a 24-player shared camp fair. The server increments it, and clients only read it. Then comes the baseline. Run stat unit, the console readout of frame timings, with fifteen NPCs chasing two clients. Read the profiler output yourself, because the fleet can run stat commands but only you can grow the reflex. Write the server frame time into docs/perf-baseline.md, the file every later measurement argues against. How does Friday's number grow into week 8's recurring gate? → concept-perf.html#gate-protocol. Done when: the kill credit increments on PlayerState and the baseline number is committed. From here on you measure. Feelings about performance are banned.

[FLEET] The moment the Bit commits, the charm burst fires. The fleet wires up the name-and-title generator word lists, the superlative kill-feed strings, and the honk sound effects, so the kill feed has jokes in it before Friday ends. The front-runner's word lists and noise are pre-authored from S2 on, so commit day is wiring, not authoring.

What this week teaches

Four ideas follow, all of them orientation. The horde's teaching home is concept-horde.html, and each idea gets one link there.

The brain in a jar. An NPC is a Pawn, a drivable body, possessed by an AIController — a brain in a jar, pluggable into any body. That one property is what the spawn tables will exploit for variety: one brain, many bodies. How does possession work, and why does it make variants nearly free? → concept-horde.html#brains

The walkable carpet. Movement runs on the NavMesh. The engine bakes a walkable carpet over the level geometry, and every pathfinding query runs against that carpet instead of the raw geometry, which is why a hundred pathfinders is even thinkable. What happens to the carpet when week 5's walls start landing on it? → concept-horde.html#navmesh

The flowchart brain. StateTree is the brain model here, a flowchart of states with enter and exit conditions, and that flowchart shape maps onto how you already think about systems. Behavior Trees, StateTree's older cousin, have far more written material, so the fallback criteria are explicit. Retreat to BTs on missing community answers or repeated editor instability, never on conceptual discomfort. What are the states, tasks, and transitions in this week's actual tree? → concept-horde.html#statetree

Body-blocking. Mobs and players share capsule collision, so blocking is free — no rigid bodies, just pure Tier 2, the cheapest slice of the physics budget. Tune capsule sizes, mass, and push interactions, and a tank role falls out of collision you already own. Why is a tank role free? → concept-horde.html#body-blocking
