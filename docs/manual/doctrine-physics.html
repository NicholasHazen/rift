Physics-legible first

When to (re)read this: before week 1 spawns its first chunk; again before the week-5 spike; and any time a new mechanic asks to be physical and you need to know what that will cost.

This doctrine rests on a belief you hold: players inherently understand simple physics problems. "Get the glowing rock through the hole; don't let the geese touch it" needs no tutorial, no tooltip, no onboarding flow — the physics is the onboarding. Rocket League is soccer with cars and is understood in four seconds. So wherever a mechanic can be expressed physically, it should be — within a budget, because "physics in multiplayer" spans from nearly-free to genuinely-heroic, and the difference is invisible from the design seat. This page is the budget, the test that spends it, and the two mechanisms that make the middle tier cheap.

The three-tier budget, and the test that spends it

The figure below draws the budget as three boxes on a rising cost curve. On the left, Tier 1 — cosmetic physics: ragdolls, debris, ducks — approximately free, because it carries no gameplay authority. In the middle, highlighted, Tier 2 — character collision: shove, carry, body-block, kick, kinematic hazards — cheap, because it rides the CharacterMovementComponent, and marked with the note that matters most: the core loop lives here. On the right, Tier 3 — true rigid bodies that players push in real time — labeled Rocket-League hard and stamped quarantined, spike-only. Same idea from the design seat; three different sports in engineering.

Tier 1 — cosmetic — is free because nobody needs agreement on where a ragdoll lands. Every client simulates its own copy, the copies disagree, and no one ever knows, because nothing true depends on the answer. Tier 2 is the gold, and here's the technical reason: none of it is actually rigid-body simulation. Body-blocking is capsule collision, nothing more — how does a capsule in a doorway become free tank design? → concept-horde.html#body-blocking. Shoving and knockback are impulses on the CharacterMovementComponent, which ships with battle-tested networked prediction. Carrying is replicated attachment. Kinematic hazards are transforms the server animates — a crusher on a timeline, not a body in a solver. A wind-up kick is a discrete, event-shaped impulse — and discrete beats continuous under latency: the wind-up hides 100 ms inside the animation, where continuous dribbling exposes every millisecond.

Tier 3 — a free-simulated body that players push in real time — is where Rocket League spent years of bespoke prediction work, and Unreal's networked physics prediction is still experimental-grade. It gets one sanctioned, hard-timeboxed spike and otherwise stays off the Stage-1 critical path.

The test, two questions

The tiers only earn their keep if you can sort a new mechanic in ten seconds, at 9pm, without a meeting. Two questions do it. First: does gameplay depend on where the thing ends up? If no — nothing true hangs on its final position — it is Tier 1: let every screen disagree, let it sleep, let it despawn. Second: do players continuously push it while it simulates? If yes — a live body under live hands — it is Tier 3, and it goes to quarantine. Everything between the two answers is Tier 2: the position matters, but the journey is either discrete (an impulse, a pop) or owned by the character (a carry, a shove) — which is why the figure marks the middle box as home.

Four boundary cases, worked

The airborne chunk. The node breaks and a chunk pops out with a little launch impulse. In flight it looks like pure spectacle — surely Tier 1. Run the test anyway: does gameplay depend on where it ends up? Yes. Someone is about to walk over and pick that rock up, and a client who joins mid-session must find it exactly where everyone else does — the resting position is gameplay authority. So the flight that produces the rest position belongs to the server: the chunk is a bounded Tier-2 pattern, server-simulated with movement replication while awake, and the whole performance is over in two seconds. Why must a rest position be a replicated fact at all? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments.

The carried chunk. Once grabbed, there is no simulation left to argue about — the chunk is a replicated attachment riding a character the CMC already predicts. Gameplay very much depends on where it is (in your hands), but nobody is pushing a simulating body, so the test lands it squarely in Tier 2 at nearly Tier-1 prices.

The two hundred ducks. Week 7's celebration command rains two hundred physics-simulated ducks onto the server (week-07.html). Does gameplay depend on where any duck ends up? No — no duck's resting spot is a fact anyone needs. Do players push them continuously? Also no. Tier 1 at scale: no gameplay authority, free to sleep, despawn, and disagree between screens — which is exactly why they work as a stress test and not as a mechanic. The ducks measure the budget; they never spend it.

The week-5 boulder. A server-simulated boulder you shove downhill into the horde: gameplay depends on where it ends up and players push it continuously while it simulates. Both questions answer expensive at once — true Tier 3, hence the quarantine. Under lag it feels like stirring soup by mail. It gets exactly one sanctioned spike, one session, hard-timeboxed (week-05.html): if the boulder sings, it's logged as a future rift-modifier ingredient; if it fights, the doctrine held at the cost of one evening. Either way it does not enter the Stage-1 critical path — the parked ambitions behind it are OQ-19.

What the doctrine does to the game

And it's a genuine upgrade, not a compromise: the node breaks into physical chunks; you grab, carry, and deliver them through a literal portal. Carrying slows you and blunts your fighting, which means encumbrance is the greed dial, physicalized — every trip to the portal plays "one more rock?" in miniature. Where does "one more rock?" become the whole hypothesis? → the-slice.html#exit-decision. Walls become tower-defense-legible barriers; a player planted in a doorway means something; shoving a goose off your chunk is real tactics. And one more bonus: comedy absorbs jank. Fall Guys ships physics wobble as charm. When Tier-2 collisions get silly, the Bit converts the failure into content.

Design memo — This doctrine also quietly de-risks week 10. Without it, legibility rides almost entirely on HUD craft; with it, the world itself teaches harvest, weight, blocking, and delivery, and the HUD only has to teach ire and the storm. Physics doesn't just add fun — it takes UI burden off the table.

The chunk contract

Loose chunks are the doctrine's one standing debt to the network, and the contract that pays it is four words long: pop → settle → sleep → net-dormant. Every loose body in the game signs it.

The figure prices the contract, station by station. Four stops run left to right — pop, settle, sleep, net-dormant — and the line underneath tracks what the chunk costs the server as it travels. At pop the line sits at its peak: the body is simulating and replicating movement, both bills at once. Settling brings it down. Sleep takes the body out of the solver. Net-dormant takes the actor off the wire entirely, and the line lands on zero — where it stays until a grab or a gust touches the chunk and the contract signs again.

Pop. When the node breaks, the server spawns the chunk, calls SetSimulatePhysics(true), gives it a small launch impulse, and turns movement replication on (SetReplicateMovement(true)) for as long as the body is awake. The server simulates the flight; clients just watch it — the airborne-chunk case above is this step, seen from the design seat.

Settle, then sleep. The simulation brings the chunk to rest in a second or two, and the physics engine puts the sleeping body out of the solver — no more simulation ticks. This step is not optional bookkeeping: replicated physics actors jitter under movement replication, so a body that never sleeps vibrates on every client's screen forever. If chunks vibrate on clients, you skipped the sleep step.

Net-dormant. SetNetDormancy(DORM_DormantAll) tells the net driver to stop considering the actor for replication at all — a sleeping chunk stops costing the server anything per tick. What do dormancy and relevancy save, in numbers? → concept-perf.html#cost-model. When anything touches a dormant chunk — a grab, a storm gust, a goose — call FlushNetDormancy() before changing replicated state, so the change actually ships.

Grab. SetSimulatePhysics(false) before AttachToComponent — a simulating body and an attachment fight over who owns the transform, and the fight looks like a chunk trembling in your hands or snapping to the floor. Kill the simulation first, then attach; the attachment itself replicates through the engine's built-in AttachmentReplication, so you replicate nothing by hand. From that moment the chunk is Tier-2 cargo on a predicted character, and the contract is dormant until it's dropped and signs again.

One number guards the whole contract: the count of live chunks per rift must be bounded, and the cap — plus what happens past it — is decided before week 8's scale pass, not during it (OQ-06). The spine below is the printable version of everything this section just said. A chunk at rest is furniture, not a simulation.

  • Pop (server): SetSimulatePhysics(true) + SetReplicateMovement(true) + small impulse
  • Settle: the simulation brings it to rest
  • Sleep: the rigid body sleeps; movement replication goes quiet
  • Net-dormant: SetNetDormancy(DORM_DormantAll)
  • Touch: FlushNetDormancy() before changing replicated state
  • Grab: SetSimulatePhysics(false) → AttachToComponent → built-in AttachmentReplication

What the CMC gives you free

Everything in this game waits its round-trip except walking. The CharacterMovementComponent is the one place Epic already spent the Rocket-League years for you: the owning client predicts its own movement locally, records saved moves, and the server validates and corrects; mispredictions rewind and replay without you writing a line of it. Client prediction, server correction, saved moves — free. That is why movement-shaped mechanics are the cheapest fun in the game, and why Tier 2 rides the CMC everywhere it can.

The free lunch has one table rule, and the waddle is where you learn it: every input to the movement math must exist identically on the server and the owning client. The predicting client computes movement from its own copies of the movement parameters. Change one on the server only — poke MaxWalkSpeed from server-side code when a player picks up a chunk — and the client keeps predicting at the old speed while the server keeps correcting it back. The symptom has a name worth memorizing before week 1: the waddle rubber-bands, which means you changed speed server-side only.

So the recipe, and it generalizes past the waddle: replicate the fact, derive the parameter. CarriedWeight is a replicated property, set by the server on grab. Speed is derived in a GetMaxSpeed() override — code that runs on the server and the owning client and computes the same answer from the same replicated fact. Never poke MaxWalkSpeed from server-only code; there is always a fact to replicate and a derivation to override instead.

Discrete pushes — the shove, the knockback, the Yeet — go through AddImpulse or LaunchCharacter on the server: event-shaped, server-initiated corrections that the CMC's machinery absorbs the same way it absorbs any correction. Two doors stay shut, one line each. Root motion — animation driving movement — networks poorly for player characters; keep player movement in the CMC's math and let animation follow it. Mover 2.0, Epic's experimental successor to the CMC, is not a mid-project migration; know it exists and walk on.

Physics-first is a sequencing bet

The only commitment this doctrine makes is build order. A Yeet is an afternoon; a taunt/threat/aggro system is a system — so the physical version ships first because it's the fastest legible version of each mechanic, not because its future is decided. Where each one goes afterward is genuinely unknown until real people play. The table below holds hypotheses, not plans — its job is to make sure the eventual decision is deliberate, not to make it early.

The five rows, spoken. The Yeet stands in for the crowd-control family — stagger, launch, resistances — and its futures run from staying forever as a refined signature verb, to folding into the ability family, to both coexisting. Body-blocking and the shove stand in for the tank kit — taunt, threat, guard abilities — and may stay raw physicality, become a class skill that keeps its physical component, or hybridize. The physical carry and its waddle stand in for inventory, carry-weight, and a Strength stat; one live future is the Deep Rock pattern — bulk goes abstract while the dramatic physical carries stay — and another is full-physical as the game's identity. Walls that block and get gnawed stand in for fortification stats, armor, and siege damage types; stats may layer on, or building destruction may turn out to be a pillar nobody planned. And physical portal delivery stands in for the extraction flow with banked inventory — the threshold ceremony may be the keeper, or physical delivery may be the extraction identity.
