The whole story, told to humans

Week 06Phase: CampBudget: 5 sessionsFun engine unlock: The Chronicle · first humansresolution: full
Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — Day 1 of the Expedition. The party grew greedy at the northern vein. Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, took exception. Nick died richest.

The timer says four minutes and the crate holds more than you can carry. You take the ferrocrystal, your friend takes the rest, and you waddle for the portal while a honking wedge crests the ridge behind you. The haul goes through the hole the old-fashioned way — chucked across the line, one armload at a time — and the channel takes you last. Then the debrief renders, the Chronicle opens with Day 1 of the Expedition, and a voice call three time zones wide goes quiet, then very loud.

By Friday you will have the whole loop running as one session — Lobby to Active to Extraction to Debrief, a portal that banks hauls, a scoreboard that ends the argument, a Chronicle that narrates the night — played end to end by three real humans on the real internet. And you will understand why the night's story travels as its own replicated event list instead of being reconstructed from state.

▣ Gate — definition of done

Three humans, remote, complete a full session — land, build, harvest, siege, extract — and receive a Chronicle that makes at least one of them laugh. Debrief notes committed. Topology: listen server over Tailscale — the first session that isn't PIE.

Six weeks in, the parts assemble into the thing itself: a session with a beginning, a middle, and an end. Land in the rift, establish the foothold, feed the machine, weather the answer, and get out through the portal with what you can carry. This is your first core belief — games are meant to be played by people — made real on the earliest possible date. Real friends. Real internet. Real stories.

The rule that gives the whole game its spine gets stated as law before anything gets built: the haul only banks when it physically crosses the threshold; die and you drop what you're carrying. Gear stays with you — haul-at-risk, gear-safe is dated law: tension, not Tarkov gear-fear. The harsher-stakes version becomes a rift-tier modifier later, for the players who want to sweat — that ladder is OQ-17's to climb. Carry is per-player and physical — dated law too — so the who-hauls-the-rares negotiation happens in the world, with your hands full, which is better than any menu could make it. The rift closes on the world's clock; the haul banks on yours. The portal is where the two clocks finally touch.

The trap this week: building the Chronicle's future instead of its v0. You'll know you're in it when a local model is downloading before the template renderer has rendered once. The move: templates and word lists ship the night; the prose-polish ambitions stay filed with the Chronicle's spec at fun-engines.html#chronicle, where they can wait without costing you Friday.

The sessions

Five sessions: a clock, a door, a ledger, a narrator, and a night. Each carries its done-when, per the week-0 convention; done-marks land as sessions close.

S1 — The clock

[NICK] The session state machine lands on the GameMode — Lobby → Active → Extraction → Debrief — with the session timer beside it. The GameMode exists only on the server, which is exactly why it gets the clock: nobody argues with a referee they can't see. Phase and time-remaining mirror onto GameState as replicated properties; the one-line authority read: the server owns and advances the phase, clients read the mirror and paint HUDs. The timer's default length is OQ-09's lean — the open-questions box below carries it. Done when: two clients walk one full phase cycle in PIE + dedicated showing the same phase and the same countdown, and nothing a client does can advance either.

S2 — The door

[NICK] The portal actor: a physical threshold that banks whatever crosses it — carried chunks, crate contents hauled over the line — plus channel-to-exit for the player and haul-drop on death. Banking runs in the server's overlap; the one-line authority read: the client watches a chunk cross, the server decides it banked. This is where encumbrance becomes the exit decision in miniature — heavy trips are slow and vulnerable, light trips waste the window — so treat the tuning as design, not plumbing: carry limits, portal placement, threshold feedback. "One more rock?" is the whole game in three words. Why does the whole design collapse into that sentence? → the-slice.html#exit-decision. Done when: a chunk banks exactly once, on the server's word, under the standing net emulation; a death drops the carry where the body fell; the channel takes the player and never the loot.

S3 — The ledgered ending

[NICK] The scoreboard, from PlayerState stats: banked, kills, ire generated, per player. Contribution visibility is the seed of fairness at 24 — the debrief prints who fed the rift, so the table never has to litigate it from memory. Then the restart flow, because Friday's table will demand "again," and the demand is the point. Done when: every client's debrief shows the same per-player rows, and "again" starts a fresh session without anyone relaunching.

S4 — The Chronicle

[NICK — by hand] The event taxonomy: which events exist and what fields each carries — first blood, biggest yeet, extractor count, deaths, who carried the most weight through the portal. It's a schema problem — your home turf — and it bounds everything the Chronicle can ever say, which is why it gets your hands. The design rule underneath: discrete events travel as a replicated event list — never reconstructed from OnRep sequences. Why do moments need their own channel? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments. Done when: the schema is committed and every notable event a PIE session emits lands in the log exactly once.

[FLEET] The event-log subsystem follows the week-4 subsystem pattern — the server appends, clients read — plus the template renderer and both tone sets, deadpan LitRPG narrator and unhinged goose-war correspondent, because OQ-11's verdict needs both candidates on stage. The awards query the same log — the four named in the payload box. Done when: a full PIE session ends in a rendered Chronicle with at least one award conferred.

S5 — The night

The platform census — run it by week 5, not Friday. Confirm which platforms your friends actually play on and where their client builds come from. The night runs on a listen server with editor builds, and this Mac only produces builds Macs can run; if a friend needs a packaged build for a platform the build host can't produce yet, run week 6 with Mac friends or slide the remote test to week 7, when the pipeline exists. Which machine can build what, and why can't this one? → concept-shipping.html#build-host The build host itself is OQ-03 — it takes your sign-off, not a fleet default.

[NICK — GUI] Everyone joins your tailnet and connects direct-IP to your listen server — Tailscale is dated law, and it defers the entire NAT-and-matchmaking problem to the week it matters. The mentor's aside below is the night's protocol — read it before the invites go out. Play the whole session and let the Chronicle do the debrief reading. Done when: three humans complete the loop, the debrief notes are written the same night and committed — the gate's last condition — and Clip #007 is whichever twenty seconds the call replays.

⚑ Fun payload — the Chronicle awakens

Player and builder at once, and the biggest payload in the manual: log every notable event during the session, then render a Chronicle at debrief — a LitRPG-chapter recap built from string templates, plus superlative awards: Most Greedy · Died Richest · Wall Enjoyer · Carried. This page's epigraph is the output spec. v0 is templates and word lists, fleet-authored; down the road, the Spark's local models polish event logs into real prose — the Chronicle is the embryo of your entire LLM-content thesis, born as a joke. Fun engine #3, and the thing your playtesters will screenshot. Its voice bible and template sets live in exactly one place → fun-engines.html#chronicle.

The Chronicle also takes a job inside this manual. Standing rule, in force from the night the first real one renders: week-page epigraphs come from actual runs — quoted, dated, attributed to their session — and run [unlogged] retires page by page as real fragments replace it. The manual's fiction becomes telemetry.

Mentor's aside — Your friends will be kind. Watch what they do, not what they say: where they hesitated, what they ignored, when they laughed, when they went quiet in the good way. The laugh timestamps are the design document. Also: schedule the session like an event with a start time. You're rehearsing the appointment-gameplay muscle you'll use all the way to seasonal launches.

What this week teaches

Three ideas, all orientation — one link each.

Facts and moments. The phase, the clock, and a wall's health are facts — state the server replicates and every client converges on. First blood and the biggest yeet are moments — things that happened once, at a time, to someone. The Chronicle is built entirely from moments, which is why the event log is its own replicated list and never a reconstruction from state changes. What breaks the night you infer the story from facts? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments

The whole cast on stage. This week deliberately employs every framework role you've met: GameMode owns the phases and exists only on the server, GameState mirrors the clock for every HUD, PlayerState carries the scoreboard stats that survive death, and the portal is one more replicated actor. If any of those assignments surprises you, re-read the casting sheet before S1. Who owns which job, and what never leaves the server? → concept-anatomy.html#cast

The listen table. Friday is the manual's first listen server: your machine is player and referee at once, which buys a free server and costs you honesty — your seat has zero latency while everyone else rides the real internet. Judge feel from a friend's screen, not your own. Which test table tells the truth about which bugs? → concept-netcode.html#topologies
