The whole story, told to humans

Week 06Phase: CampBudget: 5 sessionsFun engine unlock: The Chronicle · first humansresolution: full
Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — Day 1 of the Expedition. The party grew greedy at the northern vein. Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, took exception. Nick died richest.

The timer says four minutes and the crate holds more than you can carry. You take the ferrocrystal, your friend takes the rest, and you waddle for the portal while a honking wedge crests the ridge behind you. The haul goes through the hole the old-fashioned way — chucked across the line, one armload at a time — and the channel takes you last. Then the debrief renders, the Chronicle opens with Day 1 of the Expedition, and a voice call three time zones wide goes quiet, then very loud.

By Friday you will have the whole loop running as one session, played end to end by three real humans on the real internet: Lobby to Active to Extraction to Debrief, with a portal that banks hauls, a scoreboard that ends the argument, and a Chronicle that narrates the night. And you will understand why the night's story travels as its own replicated event list — a list the server copies to every client — instead of being reconstructed from state.

▣ Gate — definition of done

Three humans, remote, complete a full session — land, build, harvest, siege, extract — and receive a Chronicle that makes at least one of them laugh. The debrief notes are committed. The topology is a listen server — your machine hosting and playing at once — reached over Tailscale, software that puts everyone on one private network. It is the first session that isn't PIE (Play In Editor — running the game inside the editor).

Six weeks in, the parts assemble into the thing itself: a session with a beginning, a middle, and an end. You land in the rift, establish the foothold, feed the machine, weather the answer, and get out through the portal with what you can carry. And because a loop only proves itself with people inside it, this week makes your first core belief — games are meant to be played by people — real on the earliest possible date. Real friends, real internet, real stories.

Before anything gets built, the rule that gives the whole game its spine gets stated as law: the haul only banks when it physically crosses the threshold, and dying drops what you were carrying.

Gear, though, stays with you, because haul-at-risk, gear-safe is dated law, a decision already recorded in the register — the design wants tension, not Tarkov-style gear-fear. The harsher-stakes version becomes a rift-tier modifier later, for the players who want to sweat, and that ladder is OQ-17's to climb.

Carry is per-player and physical — dated law too — which means the who-hauls-the-rares negotiation happens in the world, with your hands full, and that is better than any menu could make it.

The rift closes on the world's clock; the haul banks on yours. The portal is where the two clocks finally touch.

The trap this week: building the Chronicle's future instead of its v0. You'll know you're in it when a local model is downloading before the template renderer has rendered once. The move: templates and word lists ship the night, and the prose-polish ambitions stay filed with the Chronicle's spec at fun-engines.html#chronicle, where they can wait without costing you Friday.

The sessions

Five sessions: a clock, a door, a ledger, a narrator, and a night. Each carries its done-when, per the week-0 convention; done-marks land as sessions close.

S1 — The clock

[NICK] The session state machine lands on the GameMode — the object that referees the whole match — and it cycles Lobby → Active → Extraction → Debrief with the session timer running beside it. The GameMode exists only on the server, which is exactly why it gets the clock: nobody argues with a referee they can't see. Phase and time-remaining mirror onto the GameState — the shared board every client reads — as replicated properties. Here is the one-line authority read: the server owns and advances the phase, and the clients read that mirror and paint their HUDs. The timer's default length is OQ-09's lean — the open-questions box below carries it. Done when: two clients walk one full phase cycle in PIE + dedicated (a headless server with no local player) showing the same phase and the same countdown, and nothing a client does can advance either.

S2 — The door

[NICK] S2 builds the portal actor, which is just an object the engine places into the world. That portal is a physical threshold that banks whatever crosses it — carried chunks, or the contents of a crate hauled over the line. It also channels the player out through the exit, and it drops their haul if they die on the way. Banking runs inside the server's overlap, the collision trigger the threshold fires when something enters it. Here is the one-line authority read: the client only watches a chunk cross the line, but the server is what decides it banked. This is where encumbrance becomes the exit decision in miniature: a heavy trip is slow and vulnerable, while a light trip wastes the window. So treat the tuning here as design, not plumbing — the carry limits, the portal's placement, and the feedback the threshold gives back all decide how that trip feels. "One more rock?" is the whole game in three words. Why does the whole design collapse into that sentence? → the-slice.html#exit-decision. Done when: a chunk banks exactly once, on the server's word, under the standing net emulation — the simulated lag and packet loss the editor can inject. A death drops the carry where the body fell, and the channel takes the player out but never the loot.

S3 — The ledgered ending

[NICK] The scoreboard is built from PlayerState stats — PlayerState being the per-player record the engine keeps for each of them — and it lists, for every player, what they banked, how many kills they took, and how much ire they generated. Contribution visibility is the seed of fairness at twenty-four players — the debrief prints who fed the rift, so the table never has to litigate it from memory. Then comes the restart flow, because Friday's table will demand "again," and that demand is the whole point. Done when: every client's debrief shows the same per-player rows, and "again" starts a fresh session without anyone relaunching.

S4 — The Chronicle

[NICK — by hand] First comes the event taxonomy — the catalog of which events exist and what fields each one carries. That catalog names first blood, the biggest yeet, the extractor count, the deaths, and who carried the most weight through the portal. It's a schema problem, which is your home turf, and it bounds everything the Chronicle can ever say, so it gets your hands. The design rule underneath is simple: discrete events travel as their own replicated event list. They are never reconstructed from OnRep sequences — the callbacks that fire when a replicated value lands on a client. Why do moments need their own channel? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments. Done when: the schema is committed and every notable event a PIE session emits lands in the log exactly once.

[FLEET] The event-log subsystem follows the same pattern week 4 set for subsystems — an engine-managed service where the server appends and the clients read. On top of it sit the template renderer and both tone sets. The first is a deadpan LitRPG narrator — LitRPG being fiction written in game-stat language — and the second is an unhinged goose-war correspondent. Both tones ship because OQ-11's verdict needs both candidates on stage before anyone can call it. The awards query that same log — the four of them named over in the payload box. Done when: a full PIE session ends in a rendered Chronicle with at least one award conferred.

S5 — The night

Run the platform census by week 5, not Friday. Confirm which platforms your friends actually play on, and where their client builds will come from. The night runs on a listen server with editor builds, and this PC only produces builds that Windows machines can run. That covers most of the roster, most of the time. But a Mac friend needs a client packaged on a Mac — the house has one — or else the remote test slides to week 7, when the build pipeline exists. Which machine can build what? → concept-shipping.html#build-host. The build host itself is OQ-03 — it takes your sign-off, not a fleet default.

[NICK — GUI] Everyone joins your tailnet — the private network Tailscale sets up — and connects by direct IP to your listen server. Tailscale is dated law, and it defers the whole NAT-and-matchmaking problem — the ordinary headache of getting strangers connected across the open internet — to the week that problem actually matters. The mentor's aside below is the night's protocol, so read it before the invites go out. Play the whole session, and let the Chronicle do the debrief reading. Done when: three humans complete the loop, the debrief notes are written the same night and committed — the gate's last condition — and Clip #007 is whichever twenty seconds the call replays.

⚑ Fun payload — the Chronicle awakens

Player and builder at once, and the biggest payload in the manual: log every notable event during the session, then render a Chronicle at debrief. That Chronicle is a LitRPG-chapter recap built from string templates, plus a handful of superlative awards: Most Greedy · Died Richest · Wall Enjoyer · Carried. This page's epigraph is the output spec for it. For v0 it's templates and word lists, fleet-authored. Down the road, the Spark — the little box that runs local language models — will polish those event logs into real prose, which makes the Chronicle the embryo of your entire LLM-content thesis, born here as a joke. This is fun engine #3, and it's the thing your playtesters will screenshot. Its voice bible and template sets live in exactly one place → fun-engines.html#chronicle.

The Chronicle also takes a job inside this manual. The standing rule takes force from the night the first real one renders: week-page epigraphs come from actual runs, quoted and dated and attributed to their session, and the placeholder run [unlogged] retires page by page as real fragments replace it. The manual's fiction becomes telemetry.

Mentor's aside — Your friends will be kind. Watch what they do, not what they say: where they hesitated, what they ignored, when they laughed, when they went quiet in the good way. The laugh timestamps are the design document. And schedule the session like an event, with a real start time. You're rehearsing the appointment-gameplay muscle you'll use all the way to seasonal launches.

What this week teaches

Three ideas surface this week, all of them orientation, and each gets a single link.

Facts and moments. The phase, the clock, and a wall's health are facts — state the server replicates and every client converges on. First blood and the biggest yeet are moments — things that happened once, at a time, to someone. The Chronicle is built entirely from moments, which is why the event log is its own replicated list and never a reconstruction from state changes. What breaks the night you infer the story from facts? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments

The whole cast on stage. This week deliberately employs every framework role you've met: GameMode owns the phases and exists only on the server, GameState mirrors the clock for every HUD, PlayerState carries the scoreboard stats that survive death, and the portal is one more replicated actor. If any of those assignments surprises you, re-read the casting sheet before S1. Who owns which job, and what never leaves the server? → concept-anatomy.html#cast

The listen table. Friday is the manual's first listen server, which means your machine is player and referee at once. That buys you a free server but costs you honesty: your own seat has zero latency, while everyone else rides the real internet. Judge feel from a friend's screen, not your own. Which test table tells the truth about which bugs? → concept-netcode.html#topologies
