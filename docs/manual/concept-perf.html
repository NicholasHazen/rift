Concept · Performance & the perf gate

The bill

When to (re)read this: read the cost model before week 3's Friday baseline, because that is the day the first number gets written down; read the whole page before week 8's first session, and again any Friday the gate runs red.

Twenty-four players and eighty geese sound like a design; to the server they are a bill, due every thirty-three milliseconds, forever. This page is the accounting: what each actor (the engine's word for any object in the world) costs on the wire and on the frame, the six levers that lower the bill, the lab discipline that picks the right lever, the Friday protocol that proves the bill stays paid, and the three famous tools you are not allowed to buy yet.

The cost model, once, properly

The server is the dungeon master, and performance is the price of the DM's attention. Every replicated actor in the rift — every actor whose changes the server ships to clients — is a line item on two bills at once. The first bill is the wire: every fact that changes must be shipped to every client whose copy is stale. The second bill is the frame: the server simulates the world in fixed heartbeats called ticks, and every footstep, path query, and animation pose it owes must finish before the next tick starts. Who owns the one true copy, and why do facts flow down at all? → concept-netcode.html#authority.

The wire bill has a formula, and it is worth memorizing because every network lever you will ever pull attacks exactly one of its factors: replication cost is roughly actors × properties × update frequency × recipients.

Run it with this game's own numbers. A siege at full design load is 80 NPCs (non-player characters — the horde) replicating to 24 clients, and the engine's default appetite is around 30 updates a second. So the napkin says 80 × 24 × 30 ≈ 57,600 actor-updates per second, before counting players, walls, nodes, or loose chunks, and before serializing a single property inside each update. Nobody ships that number. The point of the napkin is that the unoptimized bill is not close — it is an order-of-magnitude conversation, which is why week 8 is a week and not an afternoon. Gerald, alone in an empty rift, costs nothing worth typing; the bill is a crowd phenomenon. Where do 24 and 80 come from? → the-slice.html#three-numbers.

The figure draws the formula as four blocks, multiplied left to right, with the siege's own numbers inside. Above each block, an ember arrow names the lever that attacks it: dormancy removes actors from the conversation, Minimal mode strips properties, NetUpdateFrequency slows the clock, and relevancy trims the audience. Two levers are missing on purpose, because the anim tick and crowd following attack the other bill, the frame. The levers themselves are taught below, landmines included.

Now hold the two bills apart, because shorthand will tempt you to blur them. The formula is bandwidth: it counts what crosses the wire, and the four network levers below each attack one of its factors. The gate is CPU: server frame under 33 milliseconds, and most of a siege frame is not networking at all — it is movement, AI, and animation stacked on top of the replication work. A quiet wire proves nothing about the frame, and a green frame can hide a wire near saturation, so Friday reads both gauges. The wire and the frame are different bills.

Thirty-three milliseconds

The gate's threshold is arithmetic, not taste. A server that ticks 30 times a second has 33 milliseconds to do everything it does, and a frame that runs long doesn't get to borrow — the tick lands late, the next one later, and 24 clients feel the world thicken at once. So the budget is exactly one tick, spent in slices.

Name the slices before drawing them. Movement: the CharacterMovementComponent, the engine's built-in character mover, runs on the server for every player and every walking goose, doing both validation and simulation. AI: every brain ticks server-side — state logic, path queries, avoidance. Animation: server meshes compute poses nobody on that machine will ever see, until the anim-tick lever below turns it off. Net: the server gathers what changed, serializes it, and sends it — the CPU shadow of the wire bill. Whatever remains is headroom, and headroom is the product: it is where a spike, a spawn wave, or a bad night fits without missing the tick.

The figure draws the budget as one horizontal bar. Movement, AI, animation, and net sit left to right as labeled slices, headroom is the dark gap at the end, and the bar's right edge is a vertical line drawn in moss — 33 milliseconds, the gate, the definition of done itself. The slice widths are deliberately schematic: a way of seeing, not a measurement. Week 8's first trace under load replaces the guessed widths with real ones, and from then on the bar is something you read, not imagine.

The bar is also the diagnostic method: when the frame goes over, the first question is never "what do I optimize?" but "which slice got fat?" — and that question has an instrument, covered in the lab section below.

The six levers

If you want a day-job anchor for this whole section, reach for CDN thinking — the way a content-delivery network survives the open internet. An origin server that personally answers every request, for every client, at full freshness, dies. The fixes are edge caching, cache TTLs that decide how long a cached copy stays fresh, and simply not serving what nobody asked for. Unreal ships all three under different names. What follows is six levers in the order week 8 pulls them: four aimed at the wire's four factors and two at the frame's fattest slices, each with its landmine printed on the handle.

One — relevancy attacks recipients. NetCullDistanceSquared is edge caching, meaning clients only receive actors near enough to matter. That is the netcode page's table rule — the DM doesn't narrate events at another table — turned into a bandwidth lever (→ concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments). Your two-front siege design is secretly a relevancy optimization wearing a design hat, because split fronts mean each client is near only half the war. A sibling dial, priority, decides who gets bandwidth first when the wire is contended, and the shelf entry below covers both. The landmine is that irrelevant means nonexistent. An irrelevant actor doesn't update less often on that client; it simply isn't there. So a multicast fired from it — a message it broadcasts to every client — never arrives, and a cull distance tuned too tight makes geese pop into existence mid-charge. Tune it per class, and leave the handful of genuinely global actors — GameState, the shared match state every HUD reads — always relevant, as the engine already marks them.

Two — update frequency attacks frequency. NetUpdateFrequency is the cache TTL: a wall doesn't need 30 updates a second, but a charging elite does. Most of the world is walls. The landmine turns both ways, because too high wastes exactly the bandwidth the formula predicts, while too low makes remote movement stutter and slow-news actors feel dead the moment they matter. You have already turned this dial once, upward, because the PlayerState — the per-player record every client can read — carries its ability state faster than its scoreboard default. Why does the account need a faster clock? → concept-gas.html#asc-placement.

Three — dormancy attacks actors, and it is the strongest wire lever of all, because a dormant actor leaves the conversation entirely. The call SetNetDormancy(DORM_DormantAll) tells the net driver, the engine's replication pump, to stop considering the actor at all. Idle nodes, finished walls, and every chunk asleep on the ground are its natural customers, and the chunk contract's net-dormant step is this same lever applied as doctrine, which makes that contract dormancy's poster child. What keeps a loose chunk from becoming a simulation? → doctrine-physics.html#chunk-contract. The landmine is that server code can still mutate a dormant actor's replicated state, and the change quietly never ships. So call FlushNetDormancy() before touching it, or the wall a goose gnawed stays pristine on every client's screen.

Four — the anim tick attacks the frame's anim slice, and it is the biggest single CPU refund on the list. The server needs collision and positions, not a beautifully ticking anim graph, so NPC meshes set VisibilityBasedAnimTickOption = OnlyTickPoseWhenRendered and the pose simply stops being computed where nobody sees it. The landmine is printed in the option's own name. A dedicated server — a headless host with no local player — never renders anything, so with this option set, animation does not exist server-side. Any gameplay living in the anim graph — a notify that opens a hitbox, root motion that moves the capsule — silently never happens on the machine that owns the truth. The rule that keeps the refund safe is short: keep gameplay out of anim graphs. When a montage — a scripted animation sequence — genuinely matters to gameplay, OnlyTickMontagesWhenNotRendered exists for exactly that compromise.

Five — crowd following attacks the frame's AI slice. At siege scale, default path-following turns the wall line into a mosh pit of colliding re-paths. The fix is one component swap — CrowdFollowingComponent on the AIControllers — with the avoidance-quality dial turned down. The mechanism, the mosh pit, and the dial all live with the horde: what happens when eighty geese share one wall? → concept-horde.html#crowds. The landmine is one counterintuitive move: the dial turns down, not up. At eighty geese nobody can see the quality anyway, but the server can feel it.

Six — Minimal replication mode attacks properties. NPC ability-system components replicate Minimal: a goose's transactions never leave the server, and clients receive only the cues they can see. The ruling and its reasons live on the ledger's page: why do geese not get estates? → concept-gas.html#asc-placement. The landmine here is only forgetting — the mode is set once, where the ASC is created, and a fleet-written NPC variant that skips it quietly re-buys the whole bill.

The lab

Week 8 runs the levers as a lab, not a checklist, and the protocol matters more than the levers do. Before pulling anything, capture the baseline under load. Then write down a prediction — which lever will buy the most milliseconds — and commit that prediction to the repo right next to the baseline. Only then do you pull the levers, one at a time, measuring between each and writing every number into the repo. Being wrong about the prediction is the lesson, because the miss shows you where your model of the engine's costs is fantasy, and the correction is a better model, not a memorized trick.

The instruments come in escalating resolution. stat unit is the headline gauge, reporting frame time and roughly where it went, and it is the single number the gate reads. stat net is the wire gauge: actor counts and bandwidth, the other bill. And Unreal Insights, captured by launching the server with -trace, is the flight recorder: a full timeline of the frame that says which system ate it, instead of leaving you to guess. The frame-budget bar above stops being schematic the day of the first trace.

The answer key connects the bar to the levers. When the anim slice is the fat one, the fix is lever four. When AI is fat, reach for lever five first and then look at composition. When the net slice is fat, start with dormancy, because it removes actors from the conversation entirely, and only then work down through relevancy, frequency, and Minimal. And when movement is the fat slice, the honest answer is fewer movers — which is not a lever on this page at all, but the design's own knob, the director's hard entity cap, the one place where the perf discipline and the design agree → concept-ire.html#entity-cap. When every slice looks innocent and the frame is still fat, believe the trace over the theory. Feelings about performance are banned.

The Friday protocol

From week 8 on, Friday closes with the same experiment, run the same way, every week. The recurrence is the point. A one-time pass proves 24 worked once, but the recurring pass is what keeps 24 an engineering fact instead of a launch-day surprise. The gate's definition of done, and the week that first runs it with a bot derby to feed it, live on the week page → week-08.html. What follows is the protocol, written so an agent can run every step that doesn't need hands.

  1. Deploy the current server build to the VPS, the rented remote host the server runs on. How a build gets there → concept-shipping.html#pipeline.
  2. Load: launch the bot fleet — 20 to 24 packaged clients, each started headless with -nullrhi -nosound, auto-connecting to the server from the command line. Where the fleet lives long-term is an open question, linked below.
  3. Pressure: drive the session to siege load, 60–80 NPCs alive mid-assault. Organic greed takes an hour; the Remote forces the state in a minute → fun-engines.html#remote.
  4. Measure, sustained: stat unit through the whole siege, plus an Insights -trace captured for the archive. Sustained means the siege's worst stretch, never its best frame.
  5. Commit the numbers to docs/perf-baseline.md, the same series week 3's first baseline started. A number that isn't committed didn't happen.
  6. Verdict: green is server frame under 33 ms sustained on the VPS at that load. Red sends you back to the lab — read the trace, pick the lever, pull once, re-run.

One escalation rule keeps red Fridays honest. When the levers are spent and the frame still misses, the next move is composition and the entity cap — fewer, meaner geese is a design decision, not a defeat — and only after that story genuinely fails does the section below stop being a fence.

Not yet

Every red Friday will summon the same three names out of the forums, and all three are real, excellent, and wrong for this project right now. Read the inoculation before the first bad trace, so the midnight sales pitch lands on a decided mind.

Replication Graph replaces per-actor relevancy evaluation with a spatial graph the server maintains once for all connections — the machinery Epic built when Fortnite outgrew exactly the arithmetic in the cost model above. It is Fortnite-scale power with Fortnite-scale complexity, and the trigger for even studying it is precise: a failed perf gate that survived all six levers and the entity cap. Never speculatively. The shelf holds its doc for that day, filed as an escape hatch → shelf.html#replication-graph.

Iris is Epic's opt-in rewrite of the replication system itself — production-ready as of 5.8, and still a new engine room under the same ideas. Production-ready is not an argument; rewrites are boarded at the dock, not mid-voyage. A future project may start on Iris; this one does not switch to it.

Mass is the entity-component answer to crowds: NPCs stop being actors and become rows in tightly packed arrays, and five hundred of them get affordable. It is the 500-NPC answer, and Stage 1 asks an 80-NPC question. If a later stage designs a horde the actor model can't carry, Mass is where that conversation starts — in Stage 3, with evidence. Until a gate fails with the cap already low, the toolkit is the six levers. Tune, don't add.
