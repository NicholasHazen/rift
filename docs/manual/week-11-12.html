The fun test

This page sharpens when week 10's gate passes — do not elaborate it before then.

Chronicle fragment, run [unlogged] — The strangers wiped rich at minute sixty-one, blamed the fourth extractor, and asked when the rift reopens. Award for Unprompted Return: pending.

Sixteen strangers drop into your rift and the voice channel is all lobby chatter. By minute forty it has gone quiet — the good kind, the kind with whispered arithmetic in it — and a squad you have never met is arguing about one more rock while the storm walks toward their camp. Your cursor rests on elite.Spawn. You wait for the worst possible moment, the way any good DM does. Somewhere past the portal, the Chronicle is already taking names.

By the second Friday you will have run two structured playtests — strangers included, the build on trial as-is — and you will hold the hypothesis's verdict in writing: one honest paragraph on what's fun and what isn't. These two weeks produce signal, not features.

▣ Gate — Stage 1 exit

Two structured playtests have run. The test topology is the VPS — a server rented off your own machine — with strangers over the real internet. At least 60% of testers return unprompted, and you can write one honest paragraph on what's fun and what isn't. If the dial isn't fun yet, hear this clearly: iterating here for another month is the plan working, not failing. Stage 2 — biomes, rift modifiers, the city stub, progression — multiplies fun. It has never once created it.

Twelve weeks ago the greed dial was a paragraph of theory. Now it's a build, and these two weeks put it on trial: is the greed dial fun? Two structured Discord playtests deliver the answer — the first seats 8–12 humans with bots filling toward 24, and the second seats 16–24 humans. Between them you tune, don't add: spawn tables, ire rates, storm timing, haul weights. New features are quarantined for the duration, because a fun test contaminated by fresh bugs answers nothing.

The sessions

Two weeks, two test nights, and everything else orbits them. The per-session grid sharpens when week 10 closes, but the shape below is already firm.

Test night one — 8–12 humans, bots toward 24

You recruit these players from the OQ-15 communities, brief them in a single paragraph, and schedule the night like an event. The week-8 bot fleet fills the remaining seats toward the design target. Done when: the night ran end to end on the VPS, the survey came back, and your observation notes are written down while the memory is fresh.

Between nights — tune, don't add

You adjust every lever the first night exposed — spawn tables, ire rates, storm timing, haul weights — and nothing that isn't a lever. Done when: the diff between the two nights contains tuning files and nothing else.

Test night two — 16–24 humans

This is the full-house night, mostly human this time, and it runs the same protocol. The count that matters starts here: who came back from night one without being asked. Done when: the night ran at 16–24 humans and the return count is on paper.

The verdict

Write the one honest paragraph — what's fun, what isn't — in your own words, before the survey numbers get a chance to write it for you. It is the gate's second condition, and it is the sentence Stage 2 either builds on or waits for. Done when: the paragraph exists in the repo.

Running a playtest that produces signal

Schedule it like an event with a start time, because appointment gameplay is a muscle you'll flex all the way to seasonal launches. You first trained it on the friends nights. How does an appointment-shaped test night get run? → week-06.html. Brief players in one paragraph, maximum. During the run you observe and write, nothing else: hesitations, ignored systems, laughs, the good kind of quiet.

After each night, run a five-minute survey — four questions, no more:

  • Did you feel in control of the difficulty? — the dial, in disguise.
  • Describe your exit decision.
  • What confused you?
  • Would you play again tomorrow?

Then watch the only metric that matters: who shows up to session two unprompted.

Half the survey is already answered by the machine, because the event log knows retention, session length, and wipe causes. Chronicle data is playtest telemetry wearing a costume. Decide before night one what gets measured automatically and what only a human can tell you. Where does the event schema live? → fun-engines.html#chronicle

⚑ Fun payload — Nick, live at the Remote

Here is the party trick the whole plan has been assembling: for one session per test night, you DM the rift live. With the Director's Remote in hand, you spawn the elite at the worst possible moment, ease off when a squad's about to break, and rain ducks when morale needs it. Player: an invisible, clearly-present dungeon master is an experience nobody forgets. Builder: it is the highest-bandwidth design research available — you will feel where the automated director should push and where it should yield. Close each night with the Chronicle awards ceremony read aloud: the community's first tradition, born before the game has art. Which commands are in your hand by now? → fun-engines.html#remote
