The Fun Engines

This page is the reference floor of the Fun Mandate, where the accumulating machinery of the four engines lives alongside the closed bestiary, the Chronicle's voice, the word lists, and the debt ledger. It is documentation that ships, which means that when week 6 builds the Chronicle, the taxonomy and templates below are the build input rather than a description of it.

The Fun Mandate itself lives on the doctrine page, which is where you learn what the four engines are and why each earns permanent status. This page holds what they own instead. Why do these engines exist at all? → doctrine-fun.html#engines

The law of the page: artifacts land here the day they are born. A new Remote command ships with its row below and a changelog entry. A new JuiceKit hook joins the inventory the week it exists. A new name, title, or template joins the workspace before anything builds from it. Week pages keep their reveal prose and link in — the running totals live here and nowhere else.

The JuiceKit — the hook inventory

The JuiceKit is UJuiceComponent, born week 1: one reusable feedback component that every mechanic reports to. The standing rule it enforces: every mechanic plugs into the kit the day it is born. A mechanic without feedback isn't unfinished, it's numb, and numb is a defect. The kit lives on the client side of the authority line, because feedback is reaction rather than fact. It is what OnRep, the client's replication callback, fires when it wants to celebrate. Why do facts live on the server while celebration lives on the client? → concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments

The kit exposes four hooks, and only four — restraint is what keeps it a component instead of a subsystem, an engine-lifetime service:

  • Floating text — LitRPG combat text (raw game numbers surfaced as feedback) in mono type: +3 Ferrocrystal! drifting up from a broken node, −15 over Gerald's head.
  • Sound stinger — one-shot cues: the cha-ching, the honk, the buzzer, the klaxon.
  • Particle pop — confetti bursts, crack VFX, the small physical punctuation of an event.
  • Hitstop hook — a few frames of frozen time on impact, installed in week 1 but left dormant until week 10 tunes it.

What plugs in grows week by week, and the growth tells the project's story: the kit is born celebrating rocks in week 1, learns violence in week 2, inherits the horde's bites for free in week 3 because goose damage rides the same pipeline as player damage, gives the ire meter its voice in week 4, scores the camp's machinery in week 5, marks the portal threshold in week 6, and graduates in weeks 9 and 10. That graduation is the klaxon, the battle track, and then the tuning pass where hitstop and screen shake stop being installed and start being seasoned. The Director's Remote — the command list

The Remote is the god panel, unlocked week 4 — tuning tooling first, and by weeks 11–12 the instrument of the live-DM party trick. What earns it permanent-engine status? → doctrine-fun.html#engines. Every command executes on the server's authority — the Remote is the sanctioned way to cheat, which is exactly why it lives where the answers are made. What separates sanctioned cheating from the kind that fails safe? → concept-netcode.html#cheater-test

This list is the only complete one. A new command lands here the day it ships, with a changelog row; a command that exists anywhere else first is a filing error. There are eight commands so far, spread across three arrival weeks. ire.ReloadTuning comes first, in week 4, session 2 — three sessions before the panel itself. It arrives that early because live-reloading the tuning JSON is what makes the whole week's retune-without-recompile gate true. The core ire verbs land with the panel that Friday: ire.Give mints into the pool, ire.ForceWave makes the director spend immediately, ire.SpawnElite buys one elite outside the budget, and KillAllGeese clears the board. Week 7 adds Ducks, the load test that is also a toy. Week 9 adds the second clock's controls: storm.SetPhase and elite.Spawn, at which point you can conduct an entire session's drama by hand. The Chronicle — taxonomy, voice, and templates

The Chronicle is the end-of-run recap generated from the event log, unlocked week 6. Its v0 is string templates laid over real events. Later, local models running on the Spark, the project's local box, will polish the same log into prose. Why does it earn engine status? → doctrine-fun.html#engines. This section is the workspace that build works from: the event taxonomy, the voice bible, and the template sets are one artifact. The voice doc is the feature doc.

The event taxonomy

You own this schema. The event log is a schema-design problem, and that is home turf for you. It has two customers reading one set of rows: the ire subsystem tallies events live to price the violence, and the Chronicle reads them back at debrief to tell the story. Why one schema with two customers, and why an event log instead of reconstructing story from replication? → concept-ire.html#economy · concept-netcode.html#facts-vs-moments. The fleet writes templates against the schema, and those templates never invent fields. The families:

  • Lifecycle — session start, extraction window opens, rift closes.
  • Harvest — node broken, chunk banked, haul weight carried through the portal.
  • Combat — first blood, kills with credit, deaths with held haul, biggest yeet.
  • Build — structure placed, extractor tick, wall destroyed.
  • Ire — mint events, stage transitions, waves bought, elite purchases.

The voice bible

One narrator, fixed rules. The Chronicle is a deadpan campaign journal: it has seen worse, and it writes like it. Its laws are few enough to enumerate:

  • Past tense, always. It is a record, not a broadcast.
  • Proper nouns, always. Never "an enemy" — always Gregory. Never "a location" — always the northern vein.
  • Superlative awards close every run.
  • No adjectives it can't cash: every claim traces to a log row. If the log can't prove "heroic," the Chronicle doesn't write it.
  • Exact numbers are legal and welcome — the Chronicle is where the expedition's real ledger gets read aloud, per OQ-05's standing default.
  • It never explains its own jokes.

The two tone candidates

Two template sets get prototyped, and the playtesters' screenshots pick the winner — that verdict is OQ-11's, due when week 6 puts real Chronicles in front of real friends. The first candidate is the deadpan LitRPG narrator, the voice the bible above describes at its straightest:

The second is the unhinged goose-war correspondent — same log rows, same laws, filed from inside the siege:

The template workspace

v0 is templates over event rows plus word lists from the generators — fleet-authored, extended freely, always against the schema above. The seed grammar:

  • Opener — Day {n} of the Expedition.
  • Greed beat — The party grew greedy at {place}.
  • Reprisal beat — {elite} took exception.
  • Death line — {player} has died as they lived: {flavor}. ("holding too much Goose Down" is the canonical flavor, pending OQ-07.)
  • Awards block — superlatives from the generator list, one line each, winner named.

Local-model polish changes nothing structural when it arrives, because the Chronicle stays offline and season-time either way. Runtime LLM ambitions, meaning a language model generating text live in a session, are parked at OQ-20.

The epigraph archive

Every week page opens with a Chronicle fragment. Until week 6 those fragments are fiction, written in this voice against this bible. From week 6 on, they are replaced with real generated quotes from real runs. The manual's fiction gradually becomes telemetry. The bestiary — a closed set

Four members: Gerald, the goose hierarchy, the ducks, the traffic cone. The set is closed — no new mascots without retiring one. A cast this small is why the jokes compound instead of diluting: every clip, template, and kill-feed line draws on the same four accounts, and the interest accrues. Humor here aims at the work — the geese, the ducks, the waddle — never at the operator.

Gerald is the training dummy, hired in week 2. He comes with a nameplate, one free scream on impact, and a standing award: Employee of the Rift. Gerald absorbs the first test swing of everything: the first melee trace, the ire economy's first unit test, the storm's damage-over-time before it touches a player. His career advances at most one line per week, and only when a damage target is genuinely the tool — week 10's SFX pass ships his scream, remastered. He never gets a forced line: on the week a Gerald line would be reaching, Gerald rests.

The goose hierarchy is an org chart, not a species. The engine is middle-management bureaucracy applied to waterfowl, and it extends in exactly one direction: you invent new job titles, like Regional Goose, Goose Emeritus, or Acting Sub-Goose, but never new animals. Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm, was first over the ridge in week 3, and he is the founding member. The Bureaucrat-Goose of the Endless Storm files week 9's east-wall assault. The hierarchy is the manual's working language while the Bit, the horde's committed placeholder identity, is still on the ballot. Geese are the front-runner, but everything here survives a different winner by swapping one word list.

The ducks arrive two hundred at a time in week 7, Tier-1 cosmetic with zero gameplay authority. They exist to measure the server's budget, never to spend it, and they founded the proud tradition of load-testing tools that are also toys. Why is the flock nearly free while one shoveable boulder isn't? → doctrine-physics.html#tier-test

The traffic cone is the mannequin's hat, added in week 0 on a ten-minute timebox. It is the studio's entire art direction until week 10, and it knows it.

The Bit's identity is the open half of this section — the ballot and decision live at OQ-01, due Friday of week 3. Its retirement plan is already written: when real creature design arrives in Stage 2, the generators, the Chronicle voice, and one goose kept as a legacy elite carry over. Players will riot otherwise. The Bit is disposable; the charm systems aren't.

The generators — word lists that outlive the Bit

The generators are the charm infrastructure: word lists plus template grammars that name things. They are fleet-authored, agent-extended, and governed by the humor law, which means specificity and commitment rather than randomness. The core grammar is {name}, {rank} of {domain}, as in Gregory, Vice-Goose of the Storm. Its kill-feed form appends the verdict: {name}, {rank} of {domain}, has been slain by {player}. The joke engine is the collision of mundane names with bureaucratic rank. Extend the lists, never the mechanism.

The consumers arrive on schedule: the kill feed in week 3, the Chronicle's awards in week 6, the bot derby's twenty-four named bots in week 8, elite arrival announcements in week 9. Whether the resource names join the party — +3 Goose Down instead of placeholder taxonomy — rides OQ-07. The lists themselves ship as data like all other content in the game, JSON in, tables out. How does a text file become live game content? → concept-anatomy.html#tuning-as-text. The seed lists:

  • Names — Gregory, Harold, Brenda, Terrence, Susan, Craig, Deborah, Phillip, Janet, Gary. Mundane and committed; a goose named Blorptheus would break the engine.
  • Ranks — Vice-Goose, Bureaucrat-Goose, Regional Goose, Acting Sub-Goose, Goose Emeritus, Interim Goose, Deputy Goose, Goose (Probationary).
  • Domains — the Storm, the Endless Storm, the Northern Vein, the East Wall, the Fourth Extractor, the Quarterly Review, the Portal Line.
  • Superlatives — Most Greedy · Died Richest · Wall Enjoyer · Carried · Held the Door · One More Rock.

The fun-debt ledger

The Fun Mandate says fun is scope, not garnish, and scope needs a balance sheet. Every week ships a fun payload, one thing for the player and one for the builder. What exactly does each week's box owe? → doctrine-fun.html#payload-contract. When a week closes with its payload unshipped, the payload rolls: it lands in the next week's payload box and takes a row here, written at the Friday close. Which transaction writes it? → doctrine-os.html#friday. A row closes only when the payload ships. No row is ever cancelled: when a week runs long, stretch goals get cut and the payload doesn't, because the payload outranks them.

Each row records four things: the week the payload was owed, the payload in one line, the week it rolled to, and the date it shipped. The ledger opens empty — the table below holds the headers and waits for the first Friday that owes it a row.
